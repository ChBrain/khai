# Gift: Reference

## Line of Work

Gift as **process**: how a gift creates obligation, binds the parties, and requires a return. The domain models three phases -- giving (the transfer appears free; the giver parts with something and creates an obligation the giver cannot name), receiving (acceptance is not passive; it takes on the obligation to reciprocate; refusal refuses the relationship), reciprocating (the counter-gift completes the cycle and renews it; the interval between receiving and returning is what makes exchange a relationship rather than a transaction) -- and what each phase demands of the parties.

The spine is the sociology and anthropology of exchange: gifts are not free. Mauss establishes the triple obligation -- to give, to receive, to reciprocate -- that structures all gift exchange and distinguishes it from commodity exchange. The gift must appear free, but it creates binding social obligation; both parties must maintain the appearance of disinterest even while the obligation is real. Bourdieu deepens the analysis: misrecognition is not an illusion layered over a real transaction but a structural necessity -- the gift can only perform its social function if its obligatory character is not named. Hyde supplies the imagination dimension: what moves through the gift economy is not merely goods but spirit, creative work, the inalienable portions of persons.

## Origin

Anthropology of exchange, sociology of practice, and the gift economy.

| :------------------ | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Marcel Mauss**    | _The Gift: The Form and Reason for Exchange in Archaic Societies_ (1925; trans. 1990) | The triple obligation (to give, receive, reciprocate); the distinction between gift exchange and commodity exchange; the social function of the apparent freedom of the gift.                                                                |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Market exchange**: Commodity exchange (payment for service at an agreed equivalence) is not a gift. The market logic and the gift logic exclude each other; a gift that names its return converts itself into a transaction. This engine models gift exchange only.
- **What each party feels about the exchange**: The discomfort of a gift too large to reciprocate, the warmth of being given to -- these belong to the **emotion** and **mood** engines.
- **The status implications of giving and receiving**: Who gives to whom and in what quantity is partly governed by status; giving and receiving can move status up and down. Those dynamics belong to the **status** and **capital** engines.
- **Negotiated exchange between parties**: When the parties are explicitly negotiating the terms of an exchange, that belongs to the **negotiation** engine. Gift exchange depends on the terms not being negotiated.
