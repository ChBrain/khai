# Betrayal: Reference

## Line of Work

Betrayal as **process**: the sequence through which a persona reckons with a trust violation. The domain models four passes -- discovery (the violation is learned), disorientation (the prior map of the relationship is wrong), reckoning (what to do with the knowledge), and resolution (a new relationship to the person and the fact) -- and what each demands of the persona who is moving through it.

The spine is the interpersonal betrayal literature: betrayal as a distinct category of trust violation, defined by the dependence that makes it possible. The person who can betray is the person whose access was granted by trust; the violation uses the closeness it violates. Freyd's betrayal trauma theory supplies the awareness paradox (the betrayed person's unawareness can be adaptive when the relationship cannot be safely abandoned); Jones and Burdette supply the phenomenology (the violation of implicit or explicit expectations; the discovery process; the re-reading of the past).

The violation itself now arrives in kinds, cast from Jones and Burdette's empirical betrayal-narrative types: infidelity (the bond's own exclusivity given to another), confidence (the entrusted thing carried out of the room), and neglect (the support that did not come). A fourth kind, institutional betrayal (Smith & Freyd, 2014), sits on a different axis entirely -- not what was betrayed but who betrayed: the depended-upon institution rather than the depended-upon person, the same dependence read on a different scale. Against all four stands the blindness dial (Freyd's betrayal blindness), which sets how much of the violation the persona could see before discovery forced the account open -- the deeper the dependence, the stronger the blindness, and the harder the detonation.

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The act of betrayal from the betrayer's perspective**: The engine models the process of reckoning in the person who was betrayed. The betrayer's motivation, calculation, and response to being known belong to their own persona files and the play's context; the engine holds the victim's process.
- **Repair as the outcome**: Resolution may or may not involve repair. The process of rebuilding a relationship after a trust violation -- accounting, restitution, integration -- belongs to the **repair** engine.
- **The emotional register of betrayal**: The felt charge -- the anger, the grief, the shame -- belongs to the **emotion** and **mood** engines. The betrayal engine models the reckoning structure, not the affective load.
- **How the reckoning changes across the play**: The engine models the persona's current pass at entry. Movement across passes belongs to the play's arc.
- **The lie as such**: Deception -- what was said to conceal the violation, and how -- belongs to the **deception** engine. Betrayal reads what the lie did to the bond, never the mechanics of the lie itself.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[betrayal](process_betrayal.md)** (the root): Betrayal as process: the reckoning sequence. Anchored by Freyd (1996) -- trust violation by a dependent relationship; Jones & Burdette (1994) -- the phenomenology and structure of the reckoning.
- **[discovery](process_discovery.md)** (pass): The violation is learned; the prior account is challenged. Anchored by Jones & Burdette (1994) -- the discovery process; the gap between knowing and believing.
- **[disorientation](process_disorientation.md)** (pass): The prior map of the relationship is wrong; the past must be re-read. Anchored by Rachman (2010) -- the revision of prior account; Freyd (1996) -- what must be rebuilt when the trusted person is revealed as the violator.
- **[reckoning](process_reckoning.md)** (pass): What to do with the knowledge. Anchored by Fitness (2001) -- the script for the betrayal response; the moral accounting that follows discovery and disorientation.
- **[resolution](process_resolution.md)** (pass): A new relationship to the person and the fact. Anchored by Jones & Burdette (1994) -- the range of outcomes: repair, estrangement, private account; Freyd (1996) -- resolution without forgetting.
- **[infidelity](process_betrayal_infidelity.md)** (kind): The bond's own exclusivity given to another. Anchored by Jones & Burdette (1994) -- the most-cited betrayal-narrative type in their collections.
- **[confidence](process_betrayal_confidence.md)** (kind): The entrusted thing carried out of the room. Anchored by Jones & Burdette (1994) -- betrayed confidences among the recurring narrative types.
- **[neglect](process_betrayal_neglect.md)** (kind): The support that did not come. Anchored by Jones & Burdette (1994) -- inadequate support and ignoring among the recurring narrative types.
- **[institutional](process_betrayal_institutional.md)** (kind, who-axis): The protecting body that didn't. Anchored by Smith & Freyd (2014) -- wrongdoing by a depended-upon institution, including failure to prevent or respond supportively.
- **[blindness](position_blindness.md)** (the dial): The unawareness that protects a needed bond, scaling to the dependence. Anchored by Freyd (1996) -- betrayal trauma theory's awareness paradox; Freyd & Birrell (2013) -- betrayal blindness as a standing disposition.
