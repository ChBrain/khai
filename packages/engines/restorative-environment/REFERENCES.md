# Restorative Environment: Reference

## Line of Work

Restorative Environment as **place**: the setting that refills depleted directed attention rather than draining it. The domain models the restorative setting as anchor (what it is to be a place tuned for recovery -- what it shows, holds, offers, and withholds) and three expressions -- being-away (psychological distance from the routine that drained the attention), fascination (the soft, effortless attention the place pulls without demand), extent (a coherent world large enough to be absorbed into) -- and what each quality makes available or withholds.

The spine is Attention Restoration Theory (ART): directed attention -- the effortful, voluntary kind that holds distraction off -- is a finite resource that depletes with sustained use. A restorative environment reverses the depletion, not by adding stimulation but by requiring none: it lets directed attention rest while soft, involuntary attention does the noticing instead. Kaplan & Kaplan name four qualities a setting needs to be restorative: being away, fascination, extent, and compatibility. This engine anchors the place around all four and expresses three of them -- being-away, fascination, extent -- as the qualities most directly a matter of the setting itself; compatibility (the fit between the setting and what the persona wants to do there) is held in the anchor's Withheld, since it is not something the place supplies unilaterally.

## Origin

Environmental psychology; the cognitive theory of attention restoration.

| Source                             | Key Work                                                                                                                  | Scope                                                                                                                                                                         |
| :--------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Rachel Kaplan & Stephen Kaplan** | _The Experience of Nature: A Psychological Perspective_ (1989)                                                            | Attention Restoration Theory: directed attention as a depletable resource; the four qualities of a restorative environment -- being away, fascination, extent, compatibility. |
| **Stephen Kaplan**                 | "The Restorative Benefits of Nature: Toward an Integrative Framework," _Journal of Environmental Psychology_ 15(3) (1995) | Consolidates ART into an integrative framework; distinguishes soft fascination (compatible with reflection) from hard fascination (which crowds it out).                      |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The depletion of directed attention itself**: What drained the attention, and the mechanics of that depletion, belong to the **attention** engine. This engine models the place that refills it, not the deficit that made refilling necessary.
- **The felt calm or relief**: The subjective experience of being restored -- the sense of ease, the lightening -- belongs to the **emotion** and **mood** engines. This engine models the setting's structural pressure, not the persona's felt charge.
- **Recovery from a demand overload**: General overload from competing demands, and the recovery process that follows it, belongs to the **stress** engine. This engine models specifically the restoration of directed attention, not stress recovery in general.
