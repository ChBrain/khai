# Journey Roles: Reference

## Line of Work

Journey roles as **persona**: the recurring character functions of the hero's
journey, filled by whichever character the story needs to fill it, for as long
as the story needs that function held. The domain does not model the
journey's arc (that is the monomyth's own structure, separation, initiation,
return); it models the dramatis personae the arc recurs with, read as
functions a persona can carry rather than as fixed characters bolted to a
single name.

The spine is Campbell's monomyth cast, refined by Vogler into eight practical
archetypes for working writers: the hero, and the seven functions the hero's
road puts in front of them or beside them. A role is a mask, not a person; the
same character can wear several across a story, and the same mask can pass
between characters as the story turns.

## Origin

Comparative mythology and narrative craft, applied directly as persona
constraints: Campbell's monomyth dramatis personae as Vogler operationalized
them for story structure, with Jung's archetype theory as the ground both
draw on.

| Source                 | Key Work                                                                  | Scope                                                                                 |
| :--------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------ | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Joseph Campbell**    | _The Hero with a Thousand Faces_ (1949)                                   | The monomyth and its recurring figures across world mythology.                        |
| **Christopher Vogler** | _The Writer's Journey: Mythic Structure for Writers_ (1992; 3rd ed. 2007) | The eight character archetypes as narrative functions, not fixed characters.          |
| **Carl G. Jung**       | _The Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious_ (1959)                    | The archetypal ground the roles draw on: recurring patterns of the psyche given form. |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The specific hero**: The engine ships the function, not the character. The
  actual hero of a given production is a persona composed in the production
  itself, cast to that story's own vectors; journey-roles supplies the mask,
  never the face under it.
- **The journey's arc**: The engine models the cast, not the road. The order
  of separation, initiation, and return, and the plot beats each stage calls
  for, are the monomyth engine's; journey-roles delegates the arc entirely and
  models only who stands where along it.
- **Fixed casting**: A role is a function, not a permanent identity. Which
  persona wears which mask, and whether a persona wears more than one across a
  production (a Mentor who turns Shadow, a Shapeshifter who is also, for one
  scene, the Herald), is the play's own casting call, not something this
  engine pins in advance.

## Encoding

Source to constraint. A persona anchor (the function itself) with eight
character archetypes, Vogler's canonical eight, each a recurring role a
persona can carry.

- **[journey role](persona_journey_role.md)** (the anchor): The read itself,
  a function defined by its relation to the hero's journey, held by whichever
  character the story needs there. Anchored by Vogler (archetypes as
  functions, not fixed characters).
- **The eight archetypes** -- [hero](persona_hero.md) (the point of entry),
  [mentor](persona_mentor.md) (training and gifts), [herald](persona_herald.md)
  (the call to adventure), [threshold guardian](persona_threshold_guardian.md)
  (the test of the crossing), [shapeshifter](persona_shapeshifter.md) (loyalty
  that will not hold still), [shadow](persona_shadow.md) (the antagonist
  force), [ally](persona_ally.md) (the carried load), and
  [trickster](persona_trickster.md) (comic disruption). Anchored by Campbell
  (the monomyth's recurring figures) as refined by Vogler (the working eight),
  on Jung's archetypal ground.
