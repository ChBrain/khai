# Uncanny: Reference

## Line of Work

Uncanny as **process**: the disturbance that fires when something intimately known turns strange without ceasing to be known, or when a belief the mind surmounted long ago (that thought can act on the world, that a self might double, that the dead are not wholly gone) seems, for a moment, confirmed. The domain does not model fright in general, nor ordinary dread of a coming harm; it models the specific structure Freud identified, the return of the repressed dressed as the familiar made strange, and cuts its species directly from the sources Freud himself catalogues.

The spine is Freud's 1919 essay: the uncanny (unheimlich) is not the opposite of the familiar (heimlich) but a fold within it, since heimlich's own second sense (secret, concealed, private) already borders unheimlich; what should have stayed hidden has come to light. What returns is either a surmounted belief of the individual's own infantile life (animism, the omnipotence of thoughts) or a repressed complex (the double, castration anxiety), and either way the effect is the same: the old and discarded reasserts itself as though never given up.

## Origin

Psychoanalytic literature on the frightening-familiar, applied directly as process constraints.

| Source            | Key Work                                                                           | Scope                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    |
| :---------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Sigmund Freud** | _"Das Unheimliche"_ / _"The Uncanny"_ (1919)                                       | The spine. The uncanny as the class of the frightening that leads back to what is known of old and long familiar; the heimlich/unheimlich fold; the catalogue this engine cuts its species from: the double, involuntary repetition, animism and the omnipotence of thoughts, the animate/inanimate blur (with the castration complex read into dismembered limbs and eyes), and the return of the dead. |
| **Ernst Jentsch** | _"Zur Psychologie des Unheimlichen"_ / _"On the Psychology of the Uncanny"_ (1906) | The earlier account Freud responds to: the uncanny as intellectual uncertainty, most sharply the doubt whether a figure (a doll, an automaton) is alive or not. | **Otto Rank**     | _"Der Doppelgänger"_ (Imago, 1914)                                                 | The study of the double Freud draws on at length: the double's origin as an "energetic denial of the power of death" (primary narcissism's insurance against the ego's destruction) and its reversal, once that stage is surmounted, into the uncanny harbinger of death. Anchors the double expression.                                                                                                 |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The felt fright itself**: The engine models the specific familiar-made-strange structure, not fright, dread, or disgust in general. The raw startle, the racing pulse, the chill, is **delegated to the emotion and mood engines**. A persona may run both -- the uncanny fires on the structure of the encounter; emotion carries the charge it produces.
- **Ordinary dread or suspense**: The uncanny is not the anticipation of a coming harm. Dread holds still and waits for what might happen; the uncanny fires on something that has already, structurally, returned. Anticipatory apprehension of a bad outcome is **delegated to the emotion engine**, and where the play runs one, to a dedicated dread engine; this engine never models the wait, only the return.
- **The intellectual-uncertainty account on its own**: Jentsch's reading (is it alive, is it real) is real and is folded into the animate expression, but the engine keeps its spine on Freud's return-of-the-repressed structure rather than resting the whole domain on uncertainty alone -- an uncanny encounter here is never merely ambiguous, it is specifically the old made strange.
- **The persona's own finitude**: The revenant expression holds the flicker that the dead, specifically, are not wholly gone -- a structural disturbance about a boundary, not a stance. The standing posture a persona holds toward their own death is **delegated to the mortality engine**; that engine's territory is the wager one carries, not the shudder at a boundary crossed.
- **The work of mourning**: A revenant encounter may occur inside a bereavement, but the sustained adaptation to an irreversible loss, confronting and rebuilding by turns, is **delegated to the grief engine**. Uncanny holds the moment the boundary between living and dead wavers; grief holds the arc of living without the person on the other side of it.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[uncanny](process_uncanny.md)** (the root): the boundary process -- something known-and-put-away and something strange-and-present blur, and the persona is held in an ambivalence fright alone does not resolve. Anchored by Freud (the heimlich/unheimlich fold and the general return-of-the-repressed structure).
- **[double](process_double.md)** (expression): a self that should be singular is doubled; what began as the ego's insurance against death reverses into death's harbinger once the belief that produced it is surmounted. Anchored by Rank (the double's origin and reversal) as quoted at length by Freud.
- **[recurrence](process_recurrence.md)** (expression): the same thing happens again past what chance should allow, and the persona cannot help reading design into it. Anchored by Freud (involuntary repetition, "the constant recurrence of the same thing").
- **[animism](process_animism.md)** (expression): a surmounted belief that thought moves the world (wishing, magic) seems, for a moment, confirmed by an event that lines up with what was merely thought. Anchored by Freud (animism and the omnipotence of thoughts).
- **[animate](process_animate.md)** (expression): the animate/inanimate sort will not resolve -- a doll, a figure, a severed part acting alone -- and the not-knowing itself is where the disturbance sits. Anchored by Jentsch (intellectual uncertainty over automata) as read and partly contested by Freud, and by Freud's castration-complex reading of dismembered limbs and eyes.
- **[revenant](process_revenant.md)** (expression): the dead behave as not finished, and the persona's rational conviction that death is final meets the older, discarded belief that it might not be. Anchored by Freud (man's attitude to death and the return of the dead).
