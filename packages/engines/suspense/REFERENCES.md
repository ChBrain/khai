# Suspense: Reference

## Line of Work

Suspense as **process**: the anticipatory apprehension of a possible negative outcome -- not the emotion once it lands, but the sustained hold a persona keeps on a threat that has not yet resolved. The domain models the structure of that hold: an outcome read at high but not complete subjective certainty, a mixture of hope and fear that keeps the hold from collapsing into resignation, an interval to the outcome that is itself experienced as a cost, and a resolution at which the tension built through the hold discharges into whatever affect the outcome supplies.

The spine is Zillmann's model of suspense: an anticipatory stress reaction mediated by high but not complete subjective certainty about a threatened outcome to something the persona is invested in, sustained by "prospective emotions" -- hope and fear held together, not uncertainty alone -- and ended by excitation transfer, where slowly decaying residual arousal carries nonspecifically into the affect the outcome supplies. Loewenstein's economic account of dread supplies the second thread: the interval before a known bad outcome is itself a source of negative utility that accrues with its length, which is why a persona in suspense may act to close the distance to a worse-but-sooner outcome rather than prolong the wait.

## Origin

Suspense theory and the economics of anticipation, applied directly as process constraints.

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The felt affect once the outcome lands**: The engine models the hold before the arrival, not the arrival itself. Fear realized, relief, grief, or whatever the outcome event actually supplies belongs to the **emotion** engine; process_spillover.md ends at the discharge, not at naming what the discharge lands in.
- **The uncanny**: suspense is not the uncanny. The uncanny domain models the familiar turning strange; suspense models a temporal structure -- apprehension directed at a coming event -- regardless of whether what is feared is familiar, strange, known, or unknown. The feared outcome is routinely mundane (a diagnosis, a verdict, a confrontation); this engine does not require its object to be uncanny, and does not stage the uncanny's territory at all.
- **Generalized anxiety and mood**: suspense has an anticipated object and event -- a specific outcome the persona can, in principle, name, even where its exact timing or shape stays uncertain. Where the anticipation has no object left to name -- a diffuse, objectless dysphoria not aimed at any coming event -- the process has left suspense's territory and belongs to the **mood** engine.
- **Whether the anticipated harm is real, and what to do about it**: The engine models the holding, not the persona's agency under it. What the persona chooses to do -- confront the source, plan around it, act to change the odds -- belongs to the play's arc and to the persona's own goal, decision, and executive-function structures, not to this engine.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[suspense](process_suspense.md)** (the root): the anticipatory hold itself -- a possible harm to something invested in, read at high but not complete certainty, sustained until the outcome event answers it. Anchored by Zillmann (1996) -- suspense as anticipatory apprehension mediated by high-but-not-complete subjective certainty.
- **[narrowing](process_narrowing.md)** (facet): the read tightens toward the feared branch, alternatives closing off short of confirmation; suspense peaks not at maximal uncertainty but as certainty climbs toward the feared outcome. Anchored by Zillmann (1996) -- the certainty dimension of suspense.
- **[closing](process_closing.md)** (facet): the interval to the outcome shortens, and the shortening is itself a mounting cost rather than a relief. Anchored by Loewenstein (1987) -- dread as anticipatory disutility accruing with the length of the wait, and the preference to shorten a known bad interval over prolonging it.
- **[reprieve](process_reprieve.md)** (facet): a live hope that the feared branch will not be the one that lands, holding the hold apart from resignation. Anchored by Zillmann (1996) -- the "prospective emotions," hope and fear held together rather than uncertainty alone.
- **[spillover](process_spillover.md)** (facet): the outcome arrives or is foreclosed, and the arousal built through the hold discharges nonspecifically into whatever affect meets it. Anchored by Zillmann (1971) -- excitation-transfer theory.
