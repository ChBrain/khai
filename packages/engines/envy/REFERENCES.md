# Envy: Reference

## Line of Work

Envy as **process**: the upward comparison through which a persona reckons with another's superior good. The domain models three branches the comparison can resolve into -- benign (raise the self toward the good), malicious (lower the other who holds it), and resentment (the gap moralized into injustice) -- and what each demands of the persona running the comparison. Jealousy is deliberately not a branch: Parrott and Smith's distinction is this engine's own border, and the three-party guard of a held bond is the jealousy engine's whole domain.

The spine is social comparison and the psychology of envy. Festinger establishes that people evaluate themselves against similar others, so envy bites at the border of the self -- the near peer, not the distant great. Smith and Kim define envy as the painful awareness of another's superior quality, achievement, or possession, coupled with a longing for it or a wish that the other lacked it. Van de Ven, Zeelenberg, and Pieters split that pain into two distinct experiences -- benign envy, which moves the self up, and malicious envy, which moves the other down -- and Parrott and Smith hold envy (two-party lack) apart from jealousy (three-party fear of loss).

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The envy/gratitude character pole**: The persona's standing disposition toward the goods of others -- envious or grateful as a trait that fires under pressure -- belongs to the **virtue** engine. The envy engine models the comparison process; the stable character pole is virtue's.
- **The felt charge of the comparison**: The affective load -- the sting, the bitterness, the longing -- belongs to the **emotion** engine. The envy engine models the comparison structure, not the feeling that rides it.
- **The persona's rank**: Where the persona sits in the deference order, and which others therefore register as comparison targets at all, belongs to the **status** engine. The envy engine takes the comparison as given and models its resolution.
- **The three-party triangle**: A rival threatening a bond the persona already holds is jealousy, not envy -- two-party lack against three-party loss, Parrott and Smith's line. The whole triangle -- rival, bond, fear of loss -- is **delegated to the jealousy engine**; envy keeps only the border prose that tells the two apart.
- **The standing trait of comparing**: The persona's chronic tendency to measure the self against others is rooted in social comparison; the engine models the comparison when it is live, not the dispositional habit of running it.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[envy](process_envy.md)** (the root): Envy as process: the upward comparison at another's superior good. Anchored by Festinger (1954) -- comparison against similar others, biting at the border of the self; Smith & Kim (2007) -- envy as the painful awareness of a superior good and the longing for it.
- **[benign](process_benign.md)** (branch): The gap reads as reachable and moves the self up toward the good. Anchored by van de Ven, Zeelenberg & Pieters (2009) -- benign envy as leveling up; the comparison spent as motivation toward the good itself.
- **[malicious](process_malicious.md)** (branch): The gap reads as unfair or unreachable and moves to pull the other down. Anchored by van de Ven, Zeelenberg & Pieters (2009) -- malicious envy as leveling down; Smith & Kim (2007) -- the hostile wish that the other lacked the good.
- **[resentment](process_resentment.md)** (branch): The gap is moralized into injustice; the advantage is recoded as unfair. Anchored by Smith & Kim (2007) -- the sense of unfairness in envy and its recoding into grievance; Parrott & Smith (1993) -- the appraisal that licenses the grievance.
