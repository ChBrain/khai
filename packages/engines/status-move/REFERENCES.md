# Status Move: Reference

## Line of Work

Status move as **position**: the discrete tactical move a persona makes in the moment-by-moment negotiation of status within an interaction. The domain models the anchor (what a status move is; what it has, orders, loses, and drives) and four expressions -- ascending (the persona raises their relative status), descending (the persona lowers their own, strategically or habitually), lateral (the persona signals peer equivalence), and deflecting (the persona redirects incoming status pressure without accepting or directly contesting it) -- and what each move requires and forecloses.

The spine is Johnstone's account of status as a continuous transactional negotiation in improvisational theatre. Johnstone's central insight is that status is not a fixed attribute but a moment-by-moment enactment: every small behavior -- a hesitation, a qualification, a steady gaze, a self-interruption -- is a status transaction between specific people. Status moves are not grand gestures; they are the granular behaviors through which relative position is continuously negotiated. Goffman supplies the structural account of face-work: the protective and restorative moves through which people manage the presentation of self and handle incoming threats to face, which maps directly onto the deflecting expression. The status engine holds the persona's ongoing rank; this engine holds the specific move they are making now.

## Origin

Improvisational theatre and sociology of interaction.

| :------------------ | :------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Keith Johnstone** | _Impro: Improvisation and the Theatre_ (1979), Routledge                   | Status as continuous moment-by-moment negotiation; specific behaviors that raise and lower status; the teacher-student dynamic as a status transaction; high-status and low-status play.             |
| **Keith Johnstone** | _Impro for Storytellers_ (1999), Routledge                                 | Status transactions in narrative; status moves as the engine of scene development; the distinction between status and dominance.                                                                     |
| **Erving Goffman**  | _Interaction Ritual: Essays on Face-to-Face Behavior_ (1967), Anchor Books | Face-work as the protective and restorative moves through which people manage the presentation of self; saving face, losing face, and the interactional moves that deflect or absorb status threats. |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The persona's status rank**: How high the persona stands in a status hierarchy, and how that rank is held or changed over time, belongs to the **status** engine. The status-move engine models the specific transactional moment, not the rank.
- **The persona's felt experience of the move**: What the persona feels while making or receiving a status move belongs to the **emotion** and **mood** engines. The status-move engine models the structural transaction.
- **Power as institutional authority**: When the status move is backed by structural position, role authority, or formal power, the **power** engine holds that dimension. The status-move engine models the interactional enactment, not the institutional basis.
- **Status moves across a whole scene**: The status-move engine models discrete, addressed moves in specific moments. A persona's general orientation to status across a scene is held by the **status** engine. Do not use this engine to model a static stance.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[status-move](position_status_move.md)** (the anchor): The discrete tactical status move as a position: what it has, orders, loses, and drives. Anchored by Johnstone (1979) -- status as continuous transactional negotiation; the addressed, moment-specific nature of status moves.
- **[ascending](position_ascending.md)** (expression): The move that raises the persona's status relative to the other. Anchored by Johnstone (1979) -- specific high-status behaviors (steady gaze, unqualified assertion, territorial expansion); the difference between claiming and receiving higher status.
- **[descending](position_descending.md)** (expression): The move that lowers the persona's own status. Anchored by Johnstone (1979) -- strategic and habitual descending; the distinction between a chosen yield and a compelled posture.
- **[lateral](position_lateral.md)** (expression): The move that signals peer equivalence. Anchored by Johnstone (1979) -- status transactions at parity; Goffman (1967) -- the interactional conditions for mutual face respect.
- **[deflecting](position_deflecting.md)** (expression): The move that redirects incoming status pressure. Anchored by Goffman (1967) -- face-work as protective and restorative moves; the deflection as a move that keeps the interaction moving without absorbing the status cost.
