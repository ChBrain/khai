# Grit: Reference

## Line of Work

Grit as **position**: the persona's resting stamina toward a distant goal --
the baseline that decides whether a top-level goal survives the years it takes
to reach, not the burst of effort in any single moment. The domain reads two
facets held together: consistency of interest (the same goal chased across
years, not traded for whatever pulls hardest today) and perseverance of effort
(work that keeps landing on the goal through setback, boredom, and plateau).
Grit is about the marathon, not the sprint -- stamina toward a far goal, held
as a standing baseline rather than run as a process.

## Origin

Grit as a construct distinct from raw talent or momentary effort, and the
scale built to measure it.

| Source                                    | Key Work                                                                                                    | Scope                                                                                                                                                                                |
| :---------------------------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Angela Duckworth**                      | _Grit: The Power of Passion and Perseverance_ (2016)                                                        | Grit as passion plus perseverance for long-term goals; effort counts twice (talent x effort = skill; skill x effort = achievement); the marathon-not-sprint framing.                 |
| **Duckworth, Peterson, Matthews & Kelly** | _"Grit: Perseverance and passion for long-term goals"_, Journal of Personality and Social Psychology (2007) | The original Grit Scale and its two factors, consistency of interest and perseverance of effort, validated across cohorts including West Point cadets and the National Spelling Bee. |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The far goal itself**: Grit is the stamina held toward a goal, not the
  goal's own content or shape. What the persona is actually aiming at
  **delegates to the goal engine**. Grit says the aim survives years; goal
  says what the aim is.
- **What moves the persona to act**: Grit is a resting disposition; the force
  that actually mobilizes a given stretch of effort **delegates to the
  motivation engine**. Grit sets the odds the goal keeps its claim; motivation
  spends the effort in the moment.
- **A broad stable disposition**: Grit reads narrowly, toward one class of
  distant goal; the wider, domain-general baseline a persona arrives with and
  returns to across every kind of situation **delegates to the temperament
  engine**. Grit rides on top of temperament, it is not a sixth axis of it.
- **The if-then follow-through**: The concrete plan that turns intent into a
  single executed action -- if situation X, then behavior Y -- **delegates to
  the implementation-intention engine**. Grit is the years-long stamina that
  makes reaching for that plan worth doing again; it is not the plan itself.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[grit](position_grit.md)** (the anchor): grit as a resting stamina toward
  a distant goal, a baseline on two facets -- consistency of interest and
  perseverance of effort -- carried as a standing orientation rather than a
  single act of will. Anchored by Duckworth (2016) and Duckworth et al.
  (2007).
- **[passion](position_passion.md)** (expression): consistency of interest,
  the same top-level goal held for years rather than swapped for the next
  pursuit that shines. Anchored by Duckworth et al. (2007), the consistency-
  of-interest factor of the Grit Scale.
- **[perseverance](position_perseverance.md)** (expression): perseverance of
  effort, work that keeps landing on the goal through setback, boredom, and
  plateau. Anchored by Duckworth et al. (2007), the perseverance-of-effort
  factor of the Grit Scale, and Duckworth (2016) on effort counting twice.
