# Goal: Reference

## Line of Work

Goal as **position**: the desired state a persona is oriented toward and how they hold the pursuit. The domain models the anchor (what a goal is; what it has, orders, loses, and drives) and four expressions -- approach (oriented toward a positive outcome), avoidance (oriented away from an undesired outcome), mastery (oriented toward learning and competence development), and performance (oriented toward demonstrating competence relative to a standard) -- and what each orientation requires and forecloses.

The spine is Locke and Latham's goal-setting theory: the empirical finding, across hundreds of studies, that specific and challenging goals consistently produce higher performance than vague or easy ones, because they make the gap between current and desired state explicit and the criteria for closure clear. This supplies the structural account of what a goal is and how it motivates. Dweck's work on implicit theories and goal orientations supplies the psychological account of what a persona believes when they pursue a goal: the entity theory (ability is fixed; performance reveals it) versus the incremental theory (ability is developed; effort is the means). Elliot and McGregor's 2x2 achievement goal framework extends both: goals have both a direction (approach vs. avoidance) and a definition criterion (mastery vs. performance), and the combination determines the motivational pattern.

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Desire in the general sense**: The goal engine models a specific, structured orientation toward a target. General wanting, longing, or preference without the features of specificity and direction belongs to the **motivation** engine.
- **The specific plans for achieving the goal**: How a persona forms the if-then plans that bridge goal intention to action belongs to the **implementation-intention** engine. The goal engine holds the orientation; the plans are held elsewhere.
- **The persona's emotional response to progress or setback**: What the persona feels when moving toward or away from the goal belongs to the **emotion** and **mood** engines.
- **Competition with a specific other**: When the performance goal becomes a direct contest against a named person, the **status-move** and **status** engines hold the transactional dynamics. The performance expression models the orientation; the specific contest is interactional content.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[goal](position_goal.md)** (the anchor): The goal as a position: what it has, orders, loses, and drives. Anchored by Locke & Latham (1990, 2002) -- goal specificity, challenge, and commitment as the structural features; Elliot (1997) -- approach and avoidance as the fundamental directional distinction.
- **[approach](position_approach.md)** (expression): Oriented toward a positive outcome; promotion focus; energized by progress signals. Anchored by Elliot & Church (1997) -- approach achievement goals; Elliot & McGregor (2001) -- approach as the directional dimension of the 2x2 framework.
- **[avoidance](position_avoidance.md)** (expression): Oriented away from an undesired outcome; prevention focus; energized by threat signals. Anchored by Elliot (1997) -- avoidance achievement goals and their asymmetric, non-terminating motivational structure.
- **[mastery](position_mastery.md)** (expression): Oriented toward competence development; intrapersonal standard; incremental theory of ability. Anchored by Dweck (1999) -- learning goal orientation and the incremental theory; Elliot & McGregor (2001) -- mastery as the definitional dimension.
- **[performance](position_performance.md)** (expression): Oriented toward demonstrating competence relative to others; normative standard; entity theory of ability. Anchored by Dweck (1999) -- performance goal orientation and the entity theory; Elliot & McGregor (2001) -- performance as the definitional dimension.
