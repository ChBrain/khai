# Position: Mastery

## Taxonomy

[Goal](position_goal.md), the expression in which the persona holds a learning or development orientation: the goal is to grow, improve, or understand more fully. The mastery goal defines success as progress relative to one's own previous performance or understanding.

## Owner

- Project: khai
- Engine: goal

## Has

The persona holds an intrapersonal standard: progress is defined against the persona's own past performance, current competence, or potential. Dweck's research identifies the implicit theory that underlies the mastery orientation: the person believes that ability is incremental -- developed through effort -- rather than fixed. What the persona has is a theory of what effort means: it is the means of development, not evidence of inadequate ability.

## Orders

Engage the difficulty. The mastery goal orders the persona to treat challenging material as the domain in which learning occurs. Setback, confusion, and error are information, not verdicts; they indicate where the work is and what to attend to next. The mastery persona is ordered to stay in the difficulty rather than retreating to the already-known.

## Loses

Comparative standing. The mastery-oriented persona does not organize their attention around where they stand relative to others; they have traded this comparison for the intrapersonal comparison. What the mastery expression loses is the social reference point that the performance orientation provides: the mastery persona may be making substantial progress and simultaneously losing ground relative to others, and the goal structure does not alert them to this.

## Drives

Toward competence increase: the forward pull of understanding more, being able to do more, or having greater command over the domain. The mastery drive is self-sustaining in a way that performance orientation is not: each increment of competence opens the next level of challenge, and the learning process itself generates the motivation to continue. Dweck's research shows that mastery-oriented students are more persistent, more resilient in the face of failure, and more likely to choose challenging tasks.
