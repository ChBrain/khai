# Psychological Safety: Reference

## Line of Work

Psychological safety as **place**: the interpersonal climate that determines what a persona can risk saying. The domain models the climate's anchor (what psychological safety is; what it shows, holds, offers, and withholds) and four expressions -- high (dissent and error are unremarkable), fragile (openness is present but conditional), low (self-censorship dominates), performative (officially encouraged, structurally punished) -- and what each climate makes available or withholds.

The spine is Edmondson's theory of team psychological safety: the belief, held collectively, that the interpersonal context is safe for taking risks -- asking questions, admitting error, raising concerns, disagreeing. The belief is not individual; it is a shared perception of the team climate. Edmondson supplies the core construct and its measurement; she also distinguishes psychological safety from interpersonal trust (trust is a dyadic perception of the other; safety is a group-level perception of the context). Nembhard and Edmondson supply the organizational extension; the construct operates in teams within hierarchies, and leader behaviour is the primary predictor. Frazier et al. supply the meta-analytic evidence for what psychological safety predicts: voice, learning behavior, engagement, and team performance.

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The persona's individual felt sense of safety**: Whether the persona feels safe (their subjective experience) belongs to the **emotion** and **mood** engines. Psychological safety is a group-level climate; individual experience of that climate is separate.
- **Trust between two specific people**: Interpersonal dyadic trust belongs to the **trust** engine. Psychological safety is a team-level belief about the group context, not a belief about a specific person.
- **Power and hierarchy that produce the climate**: What leaders do to create or destroy the safety climate is governed by the **power** engine. Psychological safety describes the result; power describes the lever.
- **Speaking-up behavior itself**: What a persona actually says when they decide to take or avoid an interpersonal risk belongs to the play's content, not to this engine. The engine models the climate that makes the choice necessary.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[psychological-safety](place_psychological_safety.md)** (the anchor): The interpersonal climate as a place: what it shows, holds, offers, and withholds. Anchored by Edmondson (1999, 2018) -- the psychological safety construct; the group-level climate as the unit.
- **[high](place_high.md)** (expression): Dissent, error, and questions are unremarkable; the full range of candid contribution is available. Anchored by Edmondson (2018) -- the high-safety zone; Frazier et al. (2017) -- performance outcomes of high safety.
- **[fragile](place_fragile.md)** (expression): Openness is present under ordinary conditions but easily disrupted. Anchored by Edmondson (2018) -- the anxiety zone; the boundary between what can be said and what cannot.
- **[low](place_low.md)** (expression): Self-censorship dominates; interpersonal risk-taking is perceived as costly. Anchored by Edmondson (1999) -- learning silence in low-safety teams; Frazier et al. (2017) -- antecedents of low safety.
- **[performative](place_performative.md)** (expression): Safety is officially claimed and structurally punished; the norm and the enforcement are in contradiction. Anchored by Edmondson (2018) -- the apathy zone and the gap between declared and real climate; Nembhard & Edmondson (2006) -- leader behavior that creates the gap.
