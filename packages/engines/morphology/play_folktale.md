# Play: The Folktale

## Estate

Project khai, the morphology engine. Not one production but the template
Vladimir Propp abstracted from a hundred Russian wondertales: the invariant
skeleton every one of them shares, stripped of its particular hero, villain,
and kingdom. The play holds the skeleton; a production borrows it to tell one
tale.

## Name

The wondertale -- Propp's term for the fairy tale proper, the sub-genre his
_Morphology of the Folktale_ (1928) formalized. What the play enacts is not a
story but the morphology of a story: the fixed sequence of functions Propp
found repeats, in the same order, across tale after tale, whatever the
surface dressing.

## Arc

The function sequence, chained as plots: an initial lack or villainy opens
the tale; the hero departs to address it; a donor tests the hero and, on
passing, grants a magical agent or helper; the hero struggles with the
villain and prevails, liquidating the initial lack; the hero returns,
pursued, sometimes unrecognized; and the tale closes on recognition -- the
true hero exposed against a false claimant, ending in wedding or ascension.
Functions may be absent from a given tale -- Propp counted no wondertale
using all 31 -- but never reordered: whatever appears, appears in this
sequence.

## Company

The seven spheres of action Propp names, the roles a function can be
assigned to regardless of which named character fills it in a given telling:
the villain (who struggles against the hero), the donor (who prepares and
hands over the magical agent), the (magical) helper (who resolves the
hero's difficulty), the princess and her father (the sought-for person and
the one who assigns the difficult task), the dispatcher (who sends the hero
into the tale), the hero (who departs, reacts to the donor, and marries),
and the false hero (who makes unfounded claims to the same feat). Alongside
the spheres, the pieces the tale moves through hands -- the magical agent,
the token, the mark or brand -- and the places a hero crosses to reach --
most of all the faraway kingdom. A plot draws its cast only from here.

## Triggers

How the phases chain: villainy or a felt lack fires the hero's departure;
the donor's test gates whether the magical agent is handed over; the
struggle's victory liquidates the initial lack; the return sets up the
recognition that closes the tale. Each phase's close is the next phase's
cue -- the sequence advances by consequence, not by calendar.

## Stakes

Whether the initial lack or villainy is liquidated and the true hero is
recognized -- wedding or ascension -- or the false hero's claim stands and
the truth never surfaces. Every plot earns its place in the play by moving
this question one function closer to its answer.
