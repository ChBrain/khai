# Negotiation: Reference

## Line of Work

Negotiation as **process**: how parties with competing interests pursue agreement. The domain models four phases -- preparation (each party clarifies interests, positions, and BATNA before the table), exploration (parties surface interests beneath stated positions; the zone of possible agreement takes shape), invention (parties generate options for mutual gain before committing), resolution (a deal is selected and binding, or impasse is declared) -- and what each demands of the parties moving through it.

The spine is principled negotiation and the analytics of bargaining: negotiation is not a contest of wills between positions but a joint problem of finding deals that both parties prefer to their alternatives. Fisher, Ury & Patton supply the framework that separates positions from interests and insists on mutual gain; Raiffa supplies the analytic structure (BATNA, reservation price, zone of possible agreement) that makes the framework operational; Mnookin, Peppet & Tulumello supply the negotiator's dilemma -- the irreducible tension between creating value and claiming it -- that governs resolution.

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **What each party feels during negotiation**: The anxiety of preparation, the frustration of deadlock, the relief of closure -- these belong to the **emotion** and **mood** engines.
- **How status and power shape what parties can claim**: The power differential between parties -- who can threaten credibly, who holds the stronger BATNA -- belongs to the **power** and **status** engines.
- **The persuasion moves within negotiation**: How a party constructs an argument or appeal to shift the other's position belongs to the **persuasion** engine. Negotiation is the process; persuasion is one tool within it.
- **Long-term relational repair after a failed negotiation**: If the negotiation itself damages the relationship, that damage belongs to the **betrayal** and **repair** engines.
