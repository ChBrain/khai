# Reversal: Reference

## Line of Work

Reversal as **process**: the inversion of a situation's direction. The domain models the root (what reversal is; how it is initiated, what it moves toward, what levers it, and what it echoes) and three phases -- approach (the established direction and the persona's investment in it), turn (the pivot event that makes the prior direction untenable), and aftermath (orientation within the inverted direction) -- and what each phase requires and forecloses.

The spine is Aristotle's _peripeteia_: the reversal of fortune that is, alongside _anagnorisis_, one of the two great mechanisms of plot in tragedy. Peripeteia is not mere surprise or change; it is the inversion of a direction -- the hero's action producing the opposite of its intended effect, or the situation moving from the established trajectory to its contrary. Aristotle's analysis supplies the structural requirement: a reversal requires an established direction, a pivot, and an inverted result. Burke extends this to social situations: the reversal of expectations is one of the primary resources of human meaning-making, available in drama, rhetoric, and social interaction. Bruner's work on narrative provides the experiential account: the canonical script and its violation, the surprise that forces re-narration.

## Origin

Poetics, rhetoric, and narrative theory.

| Source            | Key Work                                                            | Scope                                                                                                                                                                    |
| :---------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------ | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Aristotle**     | _Poetics_ (~335 BCE), trans. Malcolm Heath, Penguin Classics (1996) | _Peripeteia_ as the reversal of fortune: the action produces the opposite of its intended effect; the two great mechanisms of complex plot are reversal and recognition. |
| **Kenneth Burke** | _A Grammar of Motives_ (1945), University of California Press       | The ironic reversal as a resource of human drama and rhetoric; the dialectical reversal of perspectives; transformation as the core of symbolic action.                  |
| **Jerome Bruner** | _Actual Minds, Possible Worlds_ (1986), Harvard University Press    | Narrative as the violation of a canonical script; the trouble that disrupts the expected and forces re-narration; reversal as the engine of narrative interest.          |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Surprise without inversion**: News that is unexpected but does not change the direction of the situation is not a reversal. The reversal engine requires an established trajectory and its inversion. Unexpectedness alone belongs to the **emotion** and **attention** engines.
- **Recovery back to the prior direction**: If the persona succeeds in restoring the prior trajectory, the reversal has not completed. The aftermath phase is oriented within the inverted direction, not back toward the prior one. The **repair** engine handles restoration.
- **The persona's emotional response to the inversion**: What the persona feels as the direction inverts belongs to the **emotion** and **mood** engines. The reversal engine models the structural event; the experience of it is held elsewhere.
- **Betrayal as the specific subtype**: When the reversal involves a trusted person inverting their commitment, this is governed by the **betrayal** engine. The reversal engine models the structural form; betrayal adds the relational content.
