# Instructions: Reversal

How a Playwright wires the reversal engine into a play. You wire by linking the
play's own content to the engine's files; the engine files stay untouched, they
are canon. The Roadie plumbs the engine's law into Instructions Knowledge on
deploy. Authoring guidance, not runtime content, and it does not go on tour.

## Human

- The human names the direction the situation held before the turn, the specific event or revelation that pivoted it, and the new direction the persona now faces. The human also names what the persona had invested in the prior direction -- what has been lost, not merely changed.

## Agent

- From the play's own content, a persona links the reversal phase they are in (approach, turn, or aftermath) to their Projection. The phase is where the persona currently stands in the process, not what the persona feels about the inversion.

## Collaboration

- The betrayal engine holds the specific subtype of reversal in which a trusted person inverts their commitment. The narrative engine holds how the persona re-narrates the situation after the turn. The emotion and mood engines hold the persona's felt experience of the inversion. The repair engine holds what happens if the persona attempts to restore the prior direction.

## Knowledge

- Reversal is the inversion of a situation's direction. It has three phases: approach (the direction is established and the persona moves within it; the forces that will invert it are building but not yet visible), turn (the pivot event -- the disclosure, the discovery, the consequence -- that makes the prior direction untenable and the inverted direction unavoidable), and aftermath (the persona orients within the inverted direction; the prior trajectory is foreclosed; the horizon of expectation has been revised). A reversal is not merely surprise or change; it is the specific inversion of a trajectory the persona had treated as settled.

## System

- Do not use reversal to mean unexpected. The reversal engine requires an established direction and its inversion -- the new direction must be the contrary of the prior one, not merely different from it.
- Do not model the approach phase as neutral. The approach is characterized by the persona's investment in and commitment to the prior direction; this investment is what makes the inversion a reversal rather than a transition.
- Do not model the aftermath as recovery. The aftermath is orientation within the inverted direction, not return to the prior one. If the persona restores the prior trajectory, the reversal has not completed.
- Do not edit the engine's files; wire only from the play's side.
