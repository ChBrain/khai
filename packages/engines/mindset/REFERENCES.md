# Mindset: Reference

## Line of Work

Mindset as **position**: the persona's held theory of where ability comes
from. The domain does not model a persona's actual competence, nor the
discrete feeling that fires when a result lands, nor what pushes the persona
to act at all; it models the standing belief carried into every challenge --
whether ability is a fixed amount set early, or a capacity built through
effort, strategy, and the input of others. That belief is the lens a
challenge, an effort, and a failure are each read through before any outcome
is known.

## Origin

Self-theories of ability and their behavioral consequences under challenge.

| Source              | Key Work                                                                                   | Scope                                                                                                                                                                                               |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Carol Dweck**     | _Mindset: The New Psychology of Success_ (2006)                                            | The fixed vs. growth mindset distinction: ability held as a static given versus a capacity developed through effort, and how each theory shapes the reading of challenge, effort, and failure.      |
| **Dweck & Leggett** | "A Social-Cognitive Approach to Motivation and Personality," _Psychological Review_ (1988) | The formal self-theories model: entity theory (ability is fixed) versus incremental theory (ability is malleable), and the performance-goal versus learning-goal orientations each theory predicts. |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **What drives the persona to act** (the mobilizing force): Mindset holds
  the theory of where ability comes from, not the force that spends effort
  toward a goal once the theory is set. What actually moves the persona
  **delegates to the motivation engine**. Mindset sets whether effort reads
  as evidence or as method; motivation is what makes the persona spend it.
- **The felt response to a failure** (the emotion): Mindset sets whether a
  failure is read as verdict or as information, not the shame, sting, or
  relief that fires when the result actually lands. The discrete, cause-bound
  feeling **delegates to the emotion engine** (shame, in the failure case
  most tied to a fixed mindset's verdict-reading).
- **A stable disposition toward the world** (the trait): Mindset is a theory
  about ability specifically, held and revisable; a persona's broader,
  cross-situation disposition -- open or reserved, steady or reactive --
  **delegates to the temperament engine**. Mindset colours how a temperament
  meets a test; it is not the temperament itself.
- **How a belief updates against disconfirming evidence** (belief revision):
  Mindset names which of the two theories a persona holds and what each
  theory does once held; the general mechanics of how any belief bends or
  resists under contrary evidence **delegates to the belief engine**. A
  mindset can itself be revised -- that revision runs on belief's machinery,
  not mindset's own.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[mindset](position_mindset.md)** (the anchor): mindset as a held theory
  of where ability comes from, a belief carried into every challenge rather
  than a fact about actual capability. Anchored by Dweck (the fixed/growth
  distinction) and Dweck & Leggett (the formal self-theories model).
- **[fixed](position_fixed.md)** (expression): ability held as a static
  amount set early; failure read as a verdict on the person, effort read as
  the tell of a low amount. Anchored by Dweck & Leggett's entity theory and
  its performance-goal orientation.
- **[growth](position_growth.md)** (expression): ability held as a capacity
  built through effort and strategy; failure read as information, effort
  read as the method. Anchored by Dweck & Leggett's incremental theory and
  its learning-goal orientation, and by Dweck (2006)'s growth mindset.
