# Aggression: Reference

## Line of Work

Aggression as **process**: the mobilization of hostile force into behaviour intended to harm another who is motivated to avoid that harm. The domain models the act, not the feeling -- how inputs become arousal and hostile cognition, how appraisal routes the charge, and which of five forms the force finally takes: hostile (provoked, hot, retaliatory), instrumental (harm as a cold means to an end), displaced (redirected onto a safer target than the provoker), relational (harm through the social bond), and passive (harm through withholding).

The spine is the General Aggression Model and the frustration-aggression lineage it absorbs. A single episode runs from situational and person inputs, through a present internal state (arousal, affect, cognition), to appraisal and a chosen act; the same provocation yields a different act in a different state. Onto that spine the model fixes two cross-cutting distinctions the literature insists on: the function of the harm (hostile versus instrumental, the affective hot versus the cold means-to-an-end) and the channel it travels (the body versus the social bond, in the relational and passive forms).

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The autonomic surge**: The fight response -- the flooding, the racing heart, the body's mobilization for force -- is physiology, not the act. It belongs to the **stress** engine. Aggression begins where the surge is organized into behaviour aimed at a target.
- **Anger as the felt emotion**: The heat behind a hostile act -- anger, rage, the felt charge -- belongs to the **emotion** engine. Aggression models the behaviour intended to harm, not the feeling that may accompany it; cold instrumental harm carries no anger at all.
- **The character pole**: Whether a persona is disposed toward wrath or patience -- the standing trait, the virtue or its lack -- belongs to the **virtue** engine. Aggression holds the episode, not the enduring disposition.
- **Competitive bargaining toward agreement**: Pressing hard for advantage in a negotiation is not harm aimed at another who wants to avoid it; it drives toward a deal. That belongs to the **negotiation** engine. Aggression holds force whose end is harm, not settlement.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[aggression](process_aggression.md)** (the root): Aggression as the mobilization of hostile force; inputs through internal state to a routed act. Anchored by Anderson & Bushman (2002) -- the General Aggression Model's input-state-appraisal spine.
- **[hostile](process_hostile.md)** (form): Provoked, hot, retaliatory; the harm is the aim. Anchored by Berkowitz (1989, 1993) -- aversive affect primes the readiness to aggress; Bushman & Anderson (2001) -- the affective, function-defined pole.
- **[instrumental](process_instrumental.md)** (form): Cold harm as a means to a goal. Anchored by Bushman & Anderson (2001) -- the calculated, means-to-an-end pole of the hostile/instrumental distinction.
- **[displaced](process_displaced.md)** (form): The blocked charge redirected onto a safer substitute target. Anchored by Dollard et al. (1939) -- frustration and the displacement of aggression onto reachable targets.
- **[relational](process_relational.md)** (form): Harm through the social bond, reputation, and exclusion. Anchored by Crick & Grotpeter (1995) -- aggression channelled through the manipulation of relationships and belonging.
- **[passive](process_passive.md)** (form): Harm through withholding, obstruction, and non-action. Anchored by Bushman & Anderson (2001) -- harm defined by its intended function rather than its overt form; Crick & Grotpeter (1995) -- the indirect, deniable channel.
