# Repair: Reference

## Line of Work

Repair as **process**: what a relationship undergoes when rupture is addressed rather than abandoned. The domain models four phases -- rupture (the break is acknowledged; both parties know something is damaged), accounting (the responsible party names what happened and why), restitution (concrete action addresses the harm), integration (the repair is received; the relationship is remade on new terms) -- and what each demands of the parties moving through it.

The spine is the sociology of apology and the psychology of relational repair: repair is not restoration but reconstruction; the repaired relationship includes the rupture in its history rather than erasing it. Tavuchis supplies the irreducible structure of accounting (the apology that names, accepts, and does not merely explain away); Gottman supplies the relational dynamics (what repair attempts look like in practice and why they succeed or fail); Worthington supplies the distinction between forgiveness (a change in the harmed party's stance toward the responsible party) and reconciliation (the rebuilding of the relationship, which requires both parties and which this engine models).

The domain deepens the accounting phase to castable resolution: the account given is never generic, it arrives in a form that decides the arc. Scott and Lyman's classical account types, refined by Schönbach into a four-form phase system, supply three of those forms -- concession, excuse, justification, with the fourth, refusal, staying the guilt engine's dodge rather than an account at all. Whether any account, in any form, actually lands is decided elsewhere: Gottman's sentiment override sets the dyad's reservoir of regard, the reception every repair attempt is read against.

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Betrayal reckoning (the harmed party's process)**: The engine models the repair process jointly, with emphasis on what the responsible party must do. The harmed party's internal reckoning with the violation -- discovery, disorientation, and the decision to repair rather than withdraw -- belongs to the **betrayal** engine.
- **Forgiveness as a unilateral act**: Forgiveness (the harmed party's change of stance toward the responsible party) does not require repair and is not modelled here. This engine models reconciliation -- the rebuilding of the relationship -- which requires both parties.
- **The emotional register of repair**: The felt charge of each phase -- the shame of accounting, the relief of integration, the uncertainty of restitution -- belongs to the **emotion** and **mood** engines.
- **How the repair process shifts across the play**: The engine models the persona's current phase at entry. Movement across phases belongs to the play's arc.
- **The refused account**: A responsible party who dodges the account rather than giving it in any of the three forms modelled here is not accounting at all -- that dodge is Schönbach's fourth form, refusal, and it stays the **guilt** engine's refusal channel, run by the transgressor's own inner pull to avoid payment. Note also that Scott and Lyman's justification sub-forms share lineage with Sykes and Matza's techniques of neutralization, which guilt's refusal channel carries; the seam is this: guilt runs the inner dodge that keeps the account from ever being offered, repair runs the account once it is offered, in whatever form it takes.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[repair](process_repair.md)** (the root): Repair as process: what a relationship undergoes when rupture is addressed. Anchored by Worthington (2006) -- reconciliation as a bilateral process distinct from forgiveness; Tavuchis (1991) -- the structure that makes repair possible.
- **[rupture](process_rupture.md)** (phase): The break is acknowledged; both parties know something is damaged. Anchored by Gottman & Silver (1994) -- the acknowledged rupture as the precondition for repair; Lewicki & Tomlinson (2003) -- acknowledgement as the first step.
- **[accounting](process_accounting.md)** (phase): The responsible party names what happened and why. Anchored by Tavuchis (1991) -- the apology that names and accepts; Lewicki & Tomlinson (2003) -- the accounting phase in trust repair.
- **[accounting, concession](process_accounting_concession.md)** (form): The account that fully owns it, no discount asked. Anchored by Schönbach (1980) -- concession as the first, fullest account-phase form.
- **[accounting, excuse](process_accounting_excuse.md)** (form): Responsibility deflected while the injury is conceded. Anchored by Scott & Lyman (1968) -- excuses: accident, defeasibility, scapegoating; Schönbach (1980) -- excuse as an account-phase form.
- **[accounting, justification](process_accounting_justification.md)** (form): Responsibility accepted while the injury is denied. Anchored by Scott & Lyman (1968) -- justifications: denial of injury, denial of victim, appeal to loyalties; Schönbach (1980) -- justification as an account-phase form.
- **[restitution](process_restitution.md)** (phase): Concrete action addresses the harm. Anchored by Worthington (2006) -- restitution as the bridge between accounting and integration; Lewicki & Tomlinson (2003) -- the adequacy question.
- **[integration](process_integration.md)** (phase): The repair is received; the relationship is remade on new terms. Anchored by Worthington (2006) -- reconciliation as the rebuilt relationship; Gottman & Silver (1994) -- the ongoing test of repair.
- **[sentiment override](position_sentiment_override.md)** (dial): The dyad's accumulated regard, which decides whether a repair attempt lands. Anchored by Gottman & Silver (1994) -- sentiment override as the predictor of repair-attempt success.
