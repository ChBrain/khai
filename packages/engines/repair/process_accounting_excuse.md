# Process: Accounting, Excuse

## Taxonomy

[Accounting](process_accounting.md), the form this account arrives in.

## Owner

- Project: khai
- Engine: repair

## Initiated by

The account concedes the injury and deflects the responsibility: "it happened, and it was bad, but I could not help it."
Scott and Lyman catalogued the family of moves this form runs on - appeal to accident (bad luck rather than choice), appeal to defeasibility (impaired capacity, missing information), and scapegoating (another party's provocation redirecting the cause) - each leaving the harm on the record while relocating who authored it.

## Direction

Sideways, off the responsible party and onto circumstance.
The vector does not deny that damage occurred; it moves the causal line away from a chosen self and toward something the responsible party did not control - the body, the moment, the other person in the room.
The injury stays fixed in place while the author goes soft-focus around it.

## Lever

The genuine limits on anyone's control - accidents happen, capacity is sometimes impaired, other people do provoke.
Excuse presses on those real limits, and it succeeds exactly to the degree the harmed party can believe the limit was real rather than convenient.
It buys the account-giver mercy at the price of agency: an excuse that is accepted also concedes that the responsible party was less the author of their own conduct, a discount that costs standing even as it wins forgiveness.

## Echo

A single excuse, believed, moves the account forward almost as cleanly as concession - the harmed party absorbs a smaller share of the cause into circumstance and proceeds.
Repeated, the same technique reads differently: a persona who is repeatedly not in control of their own conduct stops sounding blameless and starts sounding unreliable, and the accounting phase reopens around the pattern rather than around any one act.
