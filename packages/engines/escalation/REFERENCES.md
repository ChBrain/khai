# Escalation: Reference

## Line of Work

Escalation as **process**: how a conflict, once it tips, climbs on its own
fuel rather than staying proportional to what started it. The engine does not
model the dispute's content -- what the parties actually disagree about -- it
models the spiral: positions hardening, issues multiplying, the opposing party
narrowing to a hostile antagonist, and each retaliatory move calibrated to
land harder than the one it answers. The climb has no ceiling built into the
process itself; only something outside the loop stops the descent.

The spine is Glasl's insight that escalation moves through qualitatively
different stages, not just a quantitative rise in intensity, paired with
Pruitt, Rubin & Kim's account of the specific transformations that carry a
dispute from light to heavy: the tactics get heavier, the issues get more
general, the goal shifts from doing well to hurting the other side, and the
number of parties and stakes involved grows.

## Origin

| Source                                        | Key Work                                                        | Scope                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             |
| :-------------------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Friedrich Glasl**                           | _Confronting Conflict_ (English ed. 1999)                       | The nine-stage escalation model: hardening of positions, debate and polemics, actions over words, coalitions and images, loss of face, threat strategies, limited destructive blows, fragmentation of the enemy, and together into the abyss -- each stage harder to reverse than the one before. |
| **Dean Pruitt, Jeffrey Rubin & Sung Hee Kim** | _Social Conflict: Escalation, Stalemate, and Settlement_ (1994) | The transformations that drive escalation: light tactics give way to heavy ones, specific issues generalize, the goal shifts from doing well to hurting the other party, and few parties become many.                                                                                             |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The harm or attack itself**: A retaliatory move's actual damage -- the
  hostile force landing on another party -- is the aggression engine's. This
  engine models the calibration and the spiral around the moves, not the
  mobilized force of any single move.
- **The mending of the rupture**: Whatever de-escalation, apology, or repair a
  scene reaches for once the spiral has run is the repair engine's. This
  engine has no built-in floor or resolution; it only names how the climb
  compounds.
- **The felt anger or fear that rides the spiral**: The internal experience --
  what the escalation feels like from inside a persona appraising the other
  side's last move -- is the emotion engine's. This engine names the
  behavioral and structural pattern, not the feeling that accompanies it.
- **The trust violation that starts it**: Where a dispute's opening move is a
  broken trust rather than an ordinary disagreement, the reckoning with that
  violation is the betrayal engine's. This engine picks up once a dispute,
  from whatever source, has begun to climb.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file. One process anchor (the spiral) and three
children: two process, one position.

- **[escalation](process_escalation.md)** (the anchor): The spiral itself --
  a disagreement tips into a self-amplifying loop once a response to it
  outgrows what it answers. Anchored by Glasl (the staged climb) and Pruitt,
  Rubin & Kim (the transformations that drive it).
- **[hardening](process_hardening.md)**: Positions harden past what the
  original disagreement earned, issues proliferate past the original one,
  and tactics used to press them get heavier in step. Anchored by Glasl's
  first stage (hardening of positions) and Pruitt, Rubin & Kim's
  light-to-heavy and specific-to-general transformations.
- **[antagonist](position_antagonist.md)**: The stance a persona holds once
  the opposing party has narrowed to a single hostile motive standing in for
  the whole person. Anchored by Glasl's coalitions-and-images and
  loss-of-face stages, where the other side stops being read as a
  disagreeing party.
- **[retaliation](process_retaliation.md)**: The tit-for-tat exchange, each
  reply rounded up from the holder's own last move and rounded down from the
  other side's, so the "matching" response is never actually matched.
  Anchored by Pruitt, Rubin & Kim's doing-well-to-hurting-other
  transformation and Glasl's threat-strategy and limited-destruction stages.
