# Piece: Symbolic Capital

## Taxonomy

[Heritage](position_heritage.md)

## Owner

- Project: khai
- Engine: heritage

## Place

The persona's own piece of it: what their name does when it enters
a room, before they speak.
The honour built by a grandfather, the disgrace caused by an uncle,
the recognition the line earned in a war, the medal that hangs in
the hallway, the rumour that has not faded across the village in
forty years -- and, layered on top of all of that, the persona's
own reputation built across their working life.
The carrier of the weight changes across the eras. In the 1700s
the name was the family name above all -- noble, merchant, peasant,
each carrying its register. By the 1800s the school began to carry
weight beside the family. In the 1900s the credential
(qualifications, institution, professional standing) added a
layer, and in the 2000s the persona's public-facing profile -- the
followers, the citations, the visible track record -- became one
more layer the room reads.
The persona walks in with the inherited weight underneath and the
earned weight on top; the room sums them at the threshold.

## Load Bearing

The capital that decides whether the persona is read as themselves
or as the name they carry.
A persona with symbolic capital is trusted before they have
demonstrated; a persona without it earns the same trust at three
times the cost, and a persona with negative symbolic capital starts
the room already in deficit.
What looks like prejudice in the moment is often the symbolic
weight of the name arriving ahead of the body.
The basis of trust shifts with the century -- birth in the 1700s,
service or scholarship in the 1800s, professional record in the
1900s, public reputation in the 2000s -- but the dynamic does not.
A persona with weight is met halfway; a persona without it walks
the whole distance.

## Apparent

What shows on the persona is not announced. Symbolic capital is
what the room already knows about them before they speak.
A surname that opens a register. A village that the room places on
a map and assigns a character to. A school whose name carries a
class. A title before the name. A scandal that an older listener
remembers without saying so.
The signal changes with the era of the listener. To a listener
formed in the 1900s, the credentials and the title carry first; to
a listener formed in the 2000s, the visible public record may
carry first instead, or alongside. The persona's name passes
through whatever the listener is most fluent at reading.
The persona inherits the weight, the room reads it, and the
encounter proceeds on the difference.

## Yearbook

The arc of having symbolic capital is the slowest to build and the
quickest to lose.
A line accumulates honour over generations -- a great deed, a long
service, a public sacrifice, a name that becomes a byword for
something the culture admires. Decades, sometimes centuries.
A peak is reached when the name carries weight independent of any
person currently using it.
Then a single event -- a betrayal, a scandal, a collaboration with
the wrong power, a fraud uncovered, a war on the wrong side. The
line breaks fast. What took five generations to build can be
inverted in one season.
The arc moves with the symbolic order of its century. Lines whose
weight came from aristocratic title peaked across the 1600s and
1700s and lost most of it across the 1800s and 1900s as the orders
that backed them dissolved. Lines whose weight came from religious
office tracked the standing of the religion. Lines whose weight came
from professional excellence or public service began to outweigh
inherited title across the 1900s in much of the world. The substance
of symbolic capital does not change; what gets read as honour does,
and the arc bends with it.
Some lines recover, but the recovery is generational and rarely
returns to the original weight. The memory is sticky.
Some lines never had positive symbolic capital and operate from
neutral; some lines inherit negative weight they did not earn and
spend their whole arc trying to convert it back to neutral.
A persona can build personal reputation across their own life, but
that is not the same thing as the name the cell hands them. The
name is older than the persona and outlives them.
