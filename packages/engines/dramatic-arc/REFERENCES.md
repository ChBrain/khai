# Dramatic Arc: Reference

## Line of Work

The dramatic arc as **play**: Gustav Freytag's pyramid, the five-part
structure of a rise from an exciting force through mounting tension to a
climax, and a fall through a tragic force to a catastrophe. The domain does
not model any single production's cast, setting, or story -- those are
delegated to the play that borrows the pyramid. It models the shape a
five-act drama runs on: the sequence of acts, what fires each one, and what
each act must move.

The spine is Freytag's own analysis of classical and Shakespearean tragedy,
abstracted from the plays he studied into a reusable structure: exposition,
rising action, climax, falling action, catastrophe, each named for its
function in the pyramid rather than for any one play's content.

## Origin

Dramatic theory, applied directly as play and plot constraints: Freytag's
technique of the drama, building on Aristotle's earlier account of plot.

| Source             | Key Work                                                                                | Scope                                                                                                                                                                             |
| :----------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Gustav Freytag** | _Die Technik des Dramas_ (1863) / _Freytag's Technique of the Drama_ (Eng. trans. 1895) | The five-part pyramid: exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, catastrophe, with the exciting force and tragic force as the pieces that fire each turn.                |
| **Aristotle**      | _Poetics_ (c. 335 BCE)                                                                  | The ground Freytag builds on: a plot with a beginning, middle, and end; complication and dénouement (desis and lysis); peripeteia, the reversal of fortune the climax formalizes. |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The specific play's content**: The engine ships the pyramid's shape, not
  any production's cast, dialogue, or setting. A named play's characters and
  story are composed outside the engine, by the play that wires to this
  anchor.
- **The cultural skin**: Which conventions dress the five acts -- comedic or
  tragic register, genre trappings, staging choices -- is the consuming
  play's. The engine ships the universal structure; the production supplies
  the skin.
- **The round-trip hero's journey**: A circular departure-and-return
  structure across a single protagonist's transformation is the monomyth
  engine's, not this one. The dramatic-arc engine is linear and closes on a
  settled outcome (tragedy or comedy), not a return to a starting state.

## Encoding

Source to constraint. A play anchor (the pyramid) with five plots (the
acts), each cued by the act before it.

- **[The Drama](play_drama.md)** (the anchor): The pyramid itself -- a rise
  to a turning point and a fall from it, run as a single dramatic movement.
  Anchored by Freytag (_Die Technik des Dramas_).
- **[Exposition](plot_exposition.md)**: The introduction -- setting,
  characters, initial situation, and the exciting force that breaks the
  stasis. Anchored by Freytag's exposition and Aristotle's beginning.
- **[Rising action](plot_rising_action.md)**: The climb (Steigerung) --
  mounting complication toward the peak. Anchored by Freytag's Steigerung
  and Aristotle's complication (desis).
- **[Climax](plot_climax.md)**: The turning point (Höhepunkt) -- the
  protagonist's fortune turns. Anchored by Freytag's Höhepunkt and
  Aristotle's peripeteia.
- **[Falling action](plot_falling_action.md)**: The fall (Fall/Umkehr) --
  consequences unwind under the tragic force, toward the final suspense
  (letzte Spannung). Anchored by Freytag's Fall.
- **[Catastrophe](plot_catastrophe.md)**: The resolution (Katastrophe) --
  the protagonist's fall in tragedy, or the restoration in comedy. Anchored
  by Freytag's Katastrophe and Aristotle's dénouement (lysis).
