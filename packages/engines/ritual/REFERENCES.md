# Ritual: Reference

## Line of Work

Ritual as **process**: how collective action crosses a threshold and marks or transforms participants. The domain models four phases -- separation (participants are detached from prior status; the ritual space is constituted), liminality (participants are betwixt and between; communitas levels structure among them), effervescence (collective attention focuses and intensifies; emotional energy and solidarity are generated), incorporation (the transformation is received; participants re-enter social life in their new status) -- and what each demands of the participants and the collective moving through it.

The spine is the sociology and anthropology of ritual: ritual action constitutes and transforms social reality rather than merely operating within it. Van Gennep supplies the tripartite structure that makes transformation possible (separation, transition, aggregation); Turner deepens the liminal phase into communitas and anti-structure and shows why the leveling is the condition of transformation; Durkheim supplies the mechanism (collective effervescence) that explains how shared action generates solidarity and makes symbols sacred; Collins updates the mechanism for micro-sociology, showing how interaction rituals chain into emotional energy that individuals carry into ordinary life.

## Origin

Anthropology and sociology of ritual, collective action, and social solidarity.

| Source                | Key Work                                                  | Scope                                                                                                                                                                                                                       |
| :-------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Arnold van Gennep** | _The Rites of Passage_ (1909; English trans. 1960)        | The tripartite structure of rites of passage: separation (detachment from prior status), transition/liminality (the threshold state), aggregation/incorporation (re-entry in new status).                                   |
| **Victor Turner**     | _The Ritual Process: Structure and Anti-Structure_ (1969) | Liminality as productive suspension of structure; communitas (undifferentiated solidarity among neophytes); anti-structure as the condition for genuine transformation.                                                     |
| **Emile Durkheim**    | _The Elementary Forms of Religious Life_ (1912)           | Collective effervescence: the intensification of shared feeling when a group focuses attention on a common object; how ritual produces solidarity and charges symbols with collective meaning.                              |
| **Randall Collins**   | _Interaction Ritual Chains_ (2004)                        | The micro-sociology of ritual: bodily co-presence, focused attention, barriers to outsiders, and shared mood as the four conditions for interaction ritual; emotional energy as the output that chains rituals across time. |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The individual's felt experience of the ritual**: What it is like to be separated from prior identity, to be in the liminal state, to feel the effervescence -- these belong to the **emotion** and **mood** engines.
- **The liminality of individual transition (not collective ritual)**: When a persona is in a state of between-ness that is not structured as a ritual, that belongs to the **liminality** engine, which models individual threshold states.
- **Status change as a positional fact**: The new status produced by incorporation is held by the **status** engine, which models what that status position means going forward.
- **The play of a scene within the ritual space**: The ritual engine models the process; what characters do and say within each phase belongs to the play itself.
