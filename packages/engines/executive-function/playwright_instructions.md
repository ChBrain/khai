# Instructions: Executive Function

How a Playwright wires the executive function engine into a play. You wire by linking the play's own content to the engine's files; the engine files stay untouched, they are canon. The Roadie plumbs the engine's law into Instructions Knowledge on deploy. Authoring guidance, not runtime content, and it does not go on tour.

## Human

- The human sets the demands on the control layer: what the persona is trying to accomplish, what competes for their attention, how much is held in working memory, and what sequence of actions the goal requires.

## Agent

- From the play's own content, a persona links the executive function mode the moment runs (inhibition, updating, shifting, or planning) under its Projection. Let the mode reflect the regulatory demand the moment places on the persona. You link to the engine, you never edit it.

## Collaboration

- What competes for response selection is the attention and stress engines'. What the persona is holding in working memory belongs here. Whether the regulatory load is depleted belongs to the reward engine -- dysregulation follows depletion. Time-perception shapes how effectively planning can reach across intervals.

## Knowledge

- Executive function is the regulatory control layer: the governor that holds a goal active against interference, suppresses the competing response, refreshes what is currently relevant in working memory, and sequences action across time. Inhibition stops the prepotent response. Updating refreshes what is held. Shifting releases the current set and takes a new one. Planning holds the goal-sequence across time.

## System

- Do let executive function show its limits under load; the layer depletes under sustained demand.
- Do let failure modes appear: the prepotent response that slips through, the plan that loses its thread, the set that cannot be released.
- Do not treat executive function as a capacity either present or absent; load and depletion are the variables.
- Do not edit the engine's files; wire only from the play's side.
