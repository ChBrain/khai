# Commons: Reference

## Line of Work

Commons as **place**: a common-pool resource governed by its own users through crafted institutions. The domain models the commons as anchor (the shared place itself: what it shows, holds, offers, and withholds) and three expressions -- pool (the depletable resource stock, subtractable and finite), boundary (who is entitled to draw and who is excluded), sanction (the monitoring and graduated response that holds the rules in force) -- and what each makes available or withholds.

The spine is Elinor Ostrom's answer to Garrett Hardin's "Tragedy of the Commons" (1968). Hardin argued that a shared, unregulated resource is doomed to overexploitation: each herder's individual incentive to add one more animal to the pasture outweighs the shared cost of overgrazing, so rational self-interest drives collective ruin unless the resource is privatized or placed under centralized state control. Ostrom's fieldwork across fisheries, irrigation systems, forests, and pastures found otherwise: communities routinely avoid the tragedy by crafting their own institutions -- clearly defined boundaries, rules matched to local conditions, collective-choice arrangements that let appropriators revise those rules, monitoring, graduated sanctions, low-cost conflict resolution, and (for larger commons) nested layers of these arrangements. The commons is not doomed by its structure alone; whether it survives turns on whether its users hold the rules that govern it.

## Origin

The economics and political science of collective action, and the empirical study of self-governed common-pool resources.

| Source             | Key Work                                                                            | Scope                                                                                                                                                                                                   |
| :----------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Garrett Hardin** | "The Tragedy of the Commons," _Science_ (1968)                                      | The baseline problem this engine's institutions answer: individually rational overuse driving collective depletion of an unregulated shared resource.                                                   |
| **Elinor Ostrom**  | _Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action_ (1990) | The design principles for durable, self-organized common-pool resource governance: clearly defined boundaries, monitoring, graduated sanctions, collective-choice rules, matched to the local resource. |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The felt experience of cooperating or defecting**: Whether an appropriator feels trust, solidarity, or the pull to free-ride belongs to the **group** and **conformity** engines. This engine models the institutional structure -- the pool, the boundary, the sanction -- not what keeping or breaking the rules feels like from inside.
- **The greed that tempts overuse**: The individual appetite to take more than one's fair share belongs to the **desire** engine. This engine models what the commons offers and withholds, not the want that presses against the limit.
- **Individual standing within the group**: Where a member ranks relative to other members -- beyond the bare fact of recognized membership -- belongs to the **hierarchy** engine. This engine models entitlement to draw from the pool, not relative status among those entitled.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[commons](place_commons.md)** (the anchor): The common-pool resource as a place: what it shows, holds, offers, and withholds. Anchored by Ostrom (1990) -- the institutions that let self-governed resources persist; Hardin (1968) -- the baseline problem those institutions answer.
- **[pool](place_pool.md)** (expression): The resource stock itself -- subtractable, finite, replenishing on its own schedule. Anchored by Ostrom (1990) -- the common-pool resource's defining characteristics and the concept of sustainable yield.
- **[boundary](place_boundary.md)** (expression): Who is entitled to appropriate resource units, and who is excluded. Anchored by Ostrom (1990), Design Principle 1 -- clearly defined boundaries as the precondition for every other design principle to hold.
- **[sanction](place_sanction.md)** (expression): The monitoring and graduated response that keeps the rules enforced. Anchored by Ostrom (1990), Design Principles 4 and 5 -- monitoring by the appropriators themselves, and sanctions graduated to the severity and repetition of the violation.
