# Position: Stance, Avoidant

## Taxonomy

[Stance](position_stance.md), the posture this is one setting of.

## Owner

- Project: khai
- Engine: forgiveness

## Has

Distance as the whole strategy: the offender routed around, rooms scanned
before entering, topics steered away from the ground where the wrong sits.
The grievance is neither pressed nor released -- the persona has simply
arranged not to meet it. This is one of the two motivations the original
TRIM measured (McCullough, Rachal, Sandage, Worthington, Brown & Hight,
1998): a persona can hold heavy avoidance with light revenge, or the
reverse, and the two move on separate schedules.

## Orders

Keep the distance.
The position organizes contact: which invitations are declined, which
seats are taken, how a shared room gets crossed without a meeting of eyes.
It does not require confrontation to maintain itself -- avoidance is the
setting that asks the least of the persona and can be sustained the
longest without anyone else noticing it is a stance at all.

## Loses

Ease of ordinary contact. Whatever the offender touches becomes
logistics -- a gathering worked around, a name that changes the shape of
a sentence. The persona keeps their standing with everyone else intact by
paying it in this one, narrowed channel; the cost is invisible from
outside and constant from inside.

## Drives

On that cost: the persona either keeps paying it indefinitely, which
[withholds](process_withheld.md) the debt without ever raising the
temperature of it, or the avoidance thins as the reason for it does --
opening toward [decisional](process_decisional.md) conduct or
[emotional](process_emotional.md) warmth. Avoidant stance is not the same
disposition as a [withheld](process_withheld.md) form: withheld is what
the grievance itself is doing -- kept, live, still owed -- while avoidant
is what the persona is doing about the offender's presence. A persona can
run a low avoidant stance while still, by their own account, withholding
forgiveness outright; the two move independently.
