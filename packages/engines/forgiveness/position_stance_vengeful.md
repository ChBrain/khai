# Position: Stance, Vengeful

## Taxonomy

[Stance](position_stance.md), the posture this is one setting of.

## Owner

- Project: khai
- Engine: forgiveness

## Has

The score kept live: the wronged party's motivation to see the offender
pay, in kind or in standing, for what they did. This is the second
motivation the original TRIM measured alongside avoidance (McCullough,
Rachal, Sandage, Worthington, Brown & Hight, 1998) -- energizing and
corrosive at once, since it keeps the persona oriented toward the offender
even as it forecloses any warmer reading of them.

## Orders

Collect.
The position presses toward the offender's loss -- their comfort, their
standing, their ease -- read as owed rather than merely wished. It does
not require a plan or an act to be in force; the readiness to see the
offender suffer, rehearsed in private, is the position doing its work
whether or not it is ever spent.

## Loses

A settled account. The persona who holds this setting keeps returning to
the wrong, re-arguing what is owed, re-imagining the payment -- the
grievance stays hot rather than filed. What is lost is rest: the vengeful
stance keeps the offender present in the persona's inner life at least as
much as an ordinary bond would, on far worse terms.

## Drives

On that cost: the persona either spends the motivation, which trades the
grievance for a fresh one of the offender's own; sits on it, unspent,
which is what [withholding](process_withheld.md) the debt through revenge
rather than mere distance looks like; or the motivation recedes as the
cost outgrows it, opening toward [benevolent](position_stance_benevolent.md)
stance and, from there, whichever form the release takes. The distinction
to hold: vengeful stance is a motivation aimed at the offender -- the wish
to see them pay -- while the [withheld](process_withheld.md) form is what
the grievance itself is doing, kept rather than released. A persona can
run high revenge and still describe their forgiveness as withheld for
reasons of justice rather than of appetite; the stance names the appetite,
the form names the account.
