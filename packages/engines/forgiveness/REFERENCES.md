# Forgiveness: Reference

## Line of Work

Forgiveness as **process**: how a persona who is owed a debt for a wrong releases it, or keeps it. The domain models four readings -- decisional (a behavioural intention that changes conduct ahead of feeling), emotional (the replacement of unforgiving emotion with other-oriented emotion), withheld (the debt kept, by justice or by self-protection), and self (the persona as both offender and creditor) -- and what each gives up or holds.

The spine is the forgiveness literature in social and clinical psychology: forgiveness as a prosocial change within the wronged party, not a transaction between two. McCullough frames it as a shift in motivation -- the decline of avoidance and revenge and the return of benevolence toward the offender. Worthington separates the decision to forgive from the emotional replacement that may follow it. Enright supplies the developmental process by which a person works from grievance toward release. The throughline is that forgiveness is unilateral and intrapersonal: it belongs to the one who was wronged, asks nothing of the offender, and is distinct from the rebuilding of the relationship.

Underneath the four readings sits the stance every one of them moves through: the wronged party's motivational posture toward the offender, before any form is chosen. McCullough's original two-factor TRIM measured avoidance and revenge as the transgression-related motivations forgiveness reduces; the later TRIM-18 added benevolence as a distinct prosocial motivation, not merely the absence of the first two. Alongside the stance sits a trait dial, forgivingness -- Roberts's character-level disposition, measured by Berry and colleagues -- that sets how readily this persona's release moves at all, across offenses and offenders, independent of any single wrong.

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Reconciliation**: Forgiveness is unilateral and intrapersonal; the rebuilding of the relationship is bilateral and requires both parties. The accounting, restitution, and integration of a repaired relationship belong to the **repair** engine. This holds even where the stance reaches benevolence: goodwill toward the offender is not, on its own, the rebuilt relationship -- a persona can hold benevolent stance while never restoring access, trust, or contact, and the boundary with **repair** sits exactly there.
- **The reckoning of the violation**: The engine fires only once the wrong is known and named. The discovery, disorientation, and reckoning that precede the question of release belong to the **betrayal** engine.
- **The emotional register**: The felt charge that is held or released -- the resentment, the hostility, the grief -- belongs to the **emotion** and **mood** engines. Forgiveness models the motion of the debt, not the affect that fills it.
- **The standard the wrong is measured against**: Whether an act was a wrong at all, and how grave, is judged elsewhere -- against the **shame** engine's social standard and the **virtue** engine's character poles. The forgiveness engine takes the wrong as given and models what is done with the debt.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[forgiveness](process_forgiveness.md)** (the root): Forgiveness as the unilateral release of a held grievance. Anchored by McCullough et al. (1997) -- forgiveness as the decline of avoidance and revenge motivation; Worthington (2006) -- release that does not wait on the offender or rebuild the relationship.
- **[decisional](process_decisional.md)** (reading): The conduct changes before the feeling turns. Anchored by Worthington (2006) -- decisional forgiveness as a behavioural intention to forgo revenge and treat the offender as a person, held independently of present emotion.
- **[emotional](process_emotional.md)** (reading): Unforgiving emotion is replaced by other-oriented emotion. Anchored by Worthington (2006) -- emotional forgiveness as the juxtaposition of empathy and compassion against resentment until the negative emotion is crowded out.
- **[withheld](process_withheld.md)** (reading): The debt is kept, by justice or by self-protection. Anchored by McCullough et al. (1997) -- sustained avoidance and revenge motivation; unforgiveness as a stance, not merely the absence of forgiveness.
- **[self](process_self.md)** (reading): The persona is both offender and creditor. Anchored by Hall & Fincham (2005) -- self-forgiveness as owning the wrong while ceasing self-punishment, distinct from self-exoneration.
- **[stance](position_stance.md)** (the channel every reading moves through): The motivational posture toward the offender, in three settings. Anchored by McCullough et al. (1998) and McCullough, Root & Cohen (2006) together.
- **[stance, avoidant](position_stance_avoidant.md)** (setting): Distance as the whole strategy. Anchored by McCullough, Rachal, Sandage, Worthington, Brown & Hight (1998) -- the TRIM avoidance motivation.
- **[stance, vengeful](position_stance_vengeful.md)** (setting): The motivation to see the offender pay. Anchored by McCullough, Rachal, Sandage, Worthington, Brown & Hight (1998) -- the TRIM revenge motivation.
- **[stance, benevolent](position_stance_benevolent.md)** (setting): Goodwill held despite the wrong, not reconciliation. Anchored by McCullough, Root & Cohen (2006) -- the TRIM-18's added benevolence factor.
- **[forgivingness](position_forgivingness.md)** (the dial): The trait disposition setting how readily the whole engine moves. Anchored by Roberts (1995) -- the trait concept; Berry, Worthington, O'Connor, Parrott & Wade (2005) -- the dispositional measure.
