# Hierarchy: Reference

## Line of Work

Hierarchy as **position**: the place a structure assigns a persona in its formal order. The domain models the title grammar -- superior (holds authority over others by the structure's rule), peer (equal rank, no structural authority either way), and subordinate (subject to another's authority by title) -- and what each assignment demands of the persona who holds it. Held, not run: the engine names the assigned place, the scene names the structure.

The grammar is the irreducible minimum of structural role theory: a position is defined by its relations to others (Merton's role-set), the relations come ascribed or achieved (Linton), any organisational structure assembles from the same three building blocks (Mintzberg), and while the legitimation of authority varies, the superior/peer/subordinate relation does not (Weber) -- which keeps the title positions structure-agnostic at the grammar level.

What the engine deliberately does not model is the rest of standing: the room's read of a persona's capital belongs to the **capital** engine, whose configuration expressions (dominant, consecrated, converted, rising, downclassed, marginalized, mismatched) carry the Bourdieu warrant this engine once duplicated.

## Origin

Structural role theory, applied directly as position constraints.

| Source              | Key Work                                    | Scope                                                                                                                                                               |
| :------------------ | :------------------------------------------ | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Ralph Linton**    | _The Study of Man_ (1936)                   | The ascribed/achieved distinction and the formal grammar of role relationships within a structure.                                                                  |
| **Robert Merton**   | _Social Theory and Social Structure_ (1957) | The role-set: a position is defined by its relations to others; the superior/peer/subordinate grammar is the irreducible minimum.                                   |
| **Henry Mintzberg** | _The Structuring of Organizations_ (1979)   | Superior / peer / subordinate as the building blocks any organisational structure assembles.                                                                        |
| **Max Weber**       | _Economy and Society_ (1922)                | The threefold legitimation of authority varies, but the superior/peer/subordinate relation does not -- keeping title positions field-agnostic at the grammar level. |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The room's read of capital**: What a persona's capital configuration makes of them in a field -- dominant, consecrated, rising, downclassed, and the rest -- belongs to the **capital** engine. This engine held a duplicate of that reading and no longer does; one phenomenon, one engine.
- **The deference order**: Who is deferred to in the scene, regardless of title, belongs to the **status** engine; the titled boss nobody defers to is precisely a disagreement between that engine's read and this one's.
- **The basis of compliance**: Why anyone obeys the title -- legitimate, coercive, reward, and the other bases -- belongs to the **power** engine; this engine holds the assignment, not its enforcement.
- **How the standing arrived**: The transmission of capital and the making of a habitus are the **heritage** engine's; the structure that issues the titles is org's; the felt sting or lift of being placed is emotion's.
- **Rank change as process**: Promotion, demotion, and the loss of place are processes; this engine is position-only. The long arc of rising or falling belongs to the play's design.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file. One family: the three title positions a structure assigns.

- **[hierarchy](position_hierarchy.md)** (the anchor): The assigned place itself -- a rank fixed by the structure before the scene, held whether or not the persona could re-earn it today. Anchored by Merton (position as relations) and Linton (the assignment).
- **[superior](position_superior.md)** (expression): Holds authority over others by the structure's rule; decide where decision is required, defer where it is not. Anchored by Linton, Merton, and Mintzberg (assigned title).
- **[peer](position_peer.md)** (expression): Equal rank, no structural authority either way; coordinate without commanding. Anchored by Merton (the role-set of equals).
- **[subordinate](position_subordinate.md)** (expression): Subject to another's authority by title; carry the assignment without making it the superior's again. Anchored by Linton and Mintzberg (the line of accountability).
