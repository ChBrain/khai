# Deception: Reference

## Line of Work

Deception as **process**: the deliberate creation of a false belief in a target's mind without their consent. The domain models the root (what deception is; how it is initiated, what it moves toward, what levers it, and what it echoes) and three phases -- construction (fabricating the false impression: what to conceal, substitute, or misdirect), delivery (the communicative act that installs the false belief in the target), and maintenance (sustaining the false belief under scrutiny and over time) -- and what each phase requires and forecloses.

The spine is Buller and Burgoon's Interpersonal Deception Theory: the account of deception as a strategic, interactive process in which the deceiver manages information, behavior, and image simultaneously while the target actively seeks to assess the truthfulness of the communication. The theory's central contribution is that deception is not a one-shot act but an ongoing, adaptive management task: the deceiver and the target are both processing information about each other, and the deception succeeds or fails depending on the deceiver's ability to manage the cues that leak their state and the target's willingness to engage their detection systems. DePaulo's empirical work on deception in everyday life supplies the prevalence data and the cue evidence: people lie more frequently than they report, for a wide range of reasons, and detection is poor because the cues associated with deception are not reliable and people's detection heuristics are not calibrated to exploit them. Vrij's forensic work extends this to high-stakes deception contexts.

Four further sources warrant the phases the spine does not fully reach. Goffman's dramaturgy frames the deceiver as a performer staging a front, locating misrepresentation on a continuum with the ordinary impression management every persona conducts; the false impression is not built from nothing but from the management of a presentation. McCornack's Information Manipulation Theory specifies how a deceptive message is constructed -- by covertly violating the conversational expectations of quantity, quality, relevance, and clarity -- giving the construction phase its mechanism vocabulary. Ekman's work on leakage supplies the delivery phase's central pressure: the involuntary signals that escape the deceiver's control, and why the channels differ in what they betray. Levine's Truth-Default Theory grounds the lever the deceiver relies on throughout: the target's default presumption of honesty, abandoned only when a trigger forces them out of it -- which is why detection is poor and why a deception that avoids triggering suspicion need not be flawless to succeed.

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Honest error**: A persona who states something false but believes it to be true is not deceiving. Deception requires intent to create a false belief; sincere error lacks that intent. The **belief** engine holds how the persona's beliefs form and update.
- **Self-deception**: The persona who deceives themselves -- who holds a false belief that serves them -- is not in the deception engine's scope. The deception engine requires a target other than the deceiver.
- **The target's detection process**: What the target does to assess the truthfulness of the persona's communication belongs to the target's own engine activations -- **belief**, **trust**, **attention**. The deception engine models the deceiver's side of the interaction.
- **Betrayal as the relational consequence**: When the deception is discovered and the relational damage is the primary concern, the **betrayal** and **repair** engines hold that dimension. The deception engine models the process of deceiving; what happens when it is discovered is held elsewhere.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[deception](process_deception.md)** (the root): The deliberate creation of a false belief as a process: what initiates it, its direction, its lever, and its echo. Anchored by Buller & Burgoon (1996) -- deception as strategic, interactive management; DePaulo et al. (1996) -- the target's poor detection and the deceiver's epistemic advantage; Levine (2014, 2020) -- the truth-default the deceiver's lever exploits, abandoned only when a trigger forces the target out of it; Goffman (1959) -- the deceiver as a performer staging a front, misrepresentation on a continuum with ordinary impression management.
- **[construction](process_construction.md)** (phase): Fabricating the false impression; choosing the mechanism (falsification, concealment, misdirection). Anchored by Buller & Burgoon (1996) -- the three primary forms of deception and their distinct demands; the cognitive load of holding the truth and the alternative simultaneously; McCornack (1992) -- message construction as the covert violation of the conversational maxims of quantity, quality, relevance, and clarity.
- **[delivery](process_delivery.md)** (phase): Presenting the false impression to the target; managing the cues that leak the deception. Anchored by DePaulo et al. (1996) -- deception cues in natural settings; Vrij (2008) -- the cognitive load account and the management of nonverbal behavior during delivery; Ekman (1985) -- leakage, the involuntary signals that escape the deceiver's control and differ by channel.
- **[maintenance](process_maintenance.md)** (phase): Sustaining the false belief under scrutiny and over time; tracking the target's evolving knowledge. Anchored by Buller & Burgoon (1996) -- the adaptive, interactive nature of deception across the interaction; the compounding cost of maintenance; Levine (2014, 2020) -- the suspicion trigger the persona must keep the target from reaching as information accumulates.
