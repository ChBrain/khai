# Shame: Reference

## Line of Work

Shame as **process**: how a persona appraises exposure against a social standard, and the appraisal produces a response directed at the self or at the one who saw. The domain does not model basic emotion (that is the emotion engine's); it models the self-conscious social emotion that requires a real or imagined witness and a standard the persona holds itself to.

The spine is social appraisal theory applied to the self-referential emotions: shame, guilt, embarrassment, and humiliation are distinct appraisals of what was seen, who saw it, and whose standard was applied. Each produces a different action tendency -- collapse and hide, repair, recover, or rage.

## Origin

Self-conscious emotion theory and social psychology of shame, applied directly as process constraints.

| Source                                  | Key Work                                                              | Scope                                                                                                                   |
| :-------------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **June Price Tangney**                  | _Shame and Guilt_ (2002)                                              | Distinction between shame (global self-condemnation, hide/withdraw) and guilt (act-specific, repair-motivated).         |
| **Michael Lewis**                       | _Shame: The Exposed Self_ (1992)                                      | The structural model: self-evaluation against a standard, with attribution to the whole self (shame) or an act (guilt). |
| **Dacher Keltner &amp; Brenda Buswell** | _"Embarrassment: Its Distinct Form and Appeasement Functions"_ (1997) | Embarrassment as a distinct mild-exposure state with a recovery trajectory, not a lesser shame.                         |
| **Linda M. Brown &amp; Susan Gilligan** | _Meeting at the Crossroads_ (1992)                                    | Humiliation as power-mediated: the verdict is imposed, not arrived at; refusal and rage are the secondary responses.    |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Basic emotion**: The engine models the social self-appraisal emotions, not the discrete appraisal-driven feelings of joy, sadness, anger, fear, or disgust. Those are **delegated to the emotion engine**. A persona may run shame and anger at once -- the two engines compose, they do not replace each other.
- **Mood**: The engine models the triggered social appraisal, not the persisting background tone. A persona in a flat or irritable mood meets the shame trigger differently; that baseline is owned by the mood engine.
- **Identity defence**: The engine models the moment of exposure and its immediate resolution, not the longer-arc defence of the self-story. That territory is owned by the identity engine and the play's arc.
- **Regulation and display**: The engine models the appraisal generated, not how the persona suppresses or performs it for the room. The gap between felt and shown is delegated to the persona (Action, Tell) and to the Cultures display rules.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[shame](process_shame.md)** (the root): The social self-appraisal mechanism -- the self seen against a standard held by a real or imagined room, the gap exposed, the response aimed at concealment, repair, recovery, or rage. Anchored by Michael Lewis (self-evaluation model) and June Price Tangney (shame as global self-condemnation).
- **[guilt](process_guilt.md)** (expression): The act is condemned, not the self; repair is the action tendency. Anchored by Tangney (guilt as behaviour-focused, with lower psychological cost than shame).
- **[embarrassment](process_embarrassment.md)** (expression): Mild, public, and recoverable; the exposure is brief and the room's reading is not catastrophic. Anchored by Keltner &amp; Buswell (embarrassment as appeasement signal, distinct from shame).
- **[humiliation](process_humiliation.md)** (expression): The verdict is imposed by another's power; the persona refuses it; rage fires beneath the wound. Anchored by the power-mediated shame literature (Brown &amp; Gilligan, Miller).
