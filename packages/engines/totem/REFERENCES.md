# Totem: Reference

## Line of Work

Totem as **piece**: an object set apart as sacred that stands for a group, holding a force out of all proportion to its substance. The domain does not model private belief or the felt awe before the object (that is the belief and awe engines'); it models the _object_ -- what it concentrates, the adherent bound to revere it, and the charging and violation that make and break its sacredness.

The spine is Durkheim's totemic principle: the totem is the emblem of the clan, and the force people feel in it is society itself, made visible in a thing. The sacred is set apart from the profane by prohibition; the object is charged not by any property of its own but by the assembled group -- collective effervescence -- and it fades without renewal. Robertson Smith supplies the sacred/taboo boundary Durkheim built on; Douglas supplies the logic of purity, danger, and the pollution that a violation of the sacred releases.

## Origin

The sociology and anthropology of the sacred, applied directly as one piece with its adherent and its arc.

| Source                 | Key Work                                         | Scope                                                                                                                                                             |
| :--------------------- | :----------------------------------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Émile Durkheim**     | _The Elementary Forms of Religious Life_ (1912)  | The totemic principle: the totem as clan emblem, the sacred/profane division, and collective effervescence as what charges the object with the group's own force. |
| **Mary Douglas**       | _Purity and Danger_ (1966)                       | The symbolic ordering of the sacred and the profane; prohibition, pollution, and what a boundary violation releases.                                              |
| **W. Robertson Smith** | _Lectures on the Religion of the Semites_ (1889) | The sacred and taboo, and the object set apart by prohibition -- the account of the holy that Durkheim built the totemic principle upon.                          |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Private belief**: What a persona holds true about the sacred, and how they revise it, is the belief engine's. Totem models the object and its social charge, not the doctrine around it.
- **The felt awe**: The vastness felt before the sacred is the awe engine's; the reverence, dread, or grief at its loss are emotion's. Totem names what the object is and does; what it feels like is delegated.
- **The gathering itself**: The rite that charges the object, and the threshold it crosses, are the ritual engine's; the assembly is the place and the group. Totem ships the object collective effervescence lands on, not the ceremony that produces it.
- **The group's structure**: Who belongs, and how the collective is organised, are the group and hierarchy engines'. Totem models the emblem that stands for the "we", not the composition of the "we".

## Encoding

Source to constraint. A piece anchor (the sacred object) with one position (who reveres it) and two processes (what makes and breaks its sacredness).

- **[totem](piece_totem.md)** (the anchor): The object set apart as sacred, standing for the group with a force beyond its substance. Anchored by Durkheim (the totemic principle) and Robertson Smith (the object set apart by prohibition).
- **[adherent](position_adherent.md)** (position): The stance of one for whom the object is sacred -- membership made visible in reverence. Anchored by Durkheim (the clan and its emblem).
- **[consecration](process_consecration.md)** (process): The gathered group charges the object; collective effervescence lodged in a thing, fading without renewal. Anchored by Durkheim (collective effervescence).
- **[desecration](process_desecration.md)** (process): The prohibition is crossed and the sacred is profaned, releasing pollution and forcing the group's reaction. Anchored by Douglas (purity, danger, and boundary violation) and Durkheim (the sacred/profane line).
