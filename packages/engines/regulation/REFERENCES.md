# Regulation: Reference

## Line of Work

Regulation as **process**: how a persona intervenes on its own emotion-generative timeline, before or after the felt push arrives. The domain does not model the appraisal that generates the emotion in the first place (that is the emotion engine's); it models what the persona does to change, forestall, or hold back a feeling once a stake has met an event.

The spine is Gross's process model of emotion regulation: the emotion-generative process unfolds along a timeline -- situation, attention, appraisal, response -- and regulation is any strategy that intervenes at one of its points. Strategies that act earlier on the timeline (antecedent-focused) change the feeling itself and are generally cheaper; strategies that act after the response has formed (response-focused) change only what shows, and pay a standing cost to hold the gap open.

The process model of emotion regulation, applied directly as process constraints.

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The emotion generated**: The engine models the intervention on the process, not the appraisal that produces the feeling being intervened on. The event meeting the stake, and the felt push that follows, are **delegated to the emotion engine**. Regulation always acts on a feeling that engine would generate.
- **Executive control**: The engine models the point on the timeline where the persona acts, not the capacity that holds a regulatory goal active and inhibits a competing response long enough to execute it. Goal maintenance and response inhibition as a general faculty are **delegated to the executive-function engine**; regulation draws on that capacity without modeling it.
- **Display rules and masking**: The engine models the persona's own act of holding back or reframing a feeling, not the room's convention for which feelings may be shown at all. What a culture or setting requires to be hidden regardless of what the persona wants is **delegated to persona (Action, Tell) and to Cultures**.
- **The survival surge**: The engine models a felt emotion already under a persona's stake, not the involuntary autonomic mobilization under acute threat. Fight, flight, freeze, and fawn routing are **delegated to the stress engine**; a persona may attempt to regulate on top of a stress response, but this engine does not generate or route that surge.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[regulation](process_regulation.md)** (the root): The intervention on the persona's own emotion-generative timeline, upstream at the situation or downstream at the expression. Anchored by Gross (1998, the process model) and Gross (2015, the timeline and the cost gradient along it).
- **[situation selection](process_situation_selection.md)** (expression): Approaching or avoiding a situation before it can generate the feeling at all. Anchored by Gross (1998, situation selection, the earliest family on the timeline).
- **[reappraisal](process_reappraisal.md)** (expression): Reframing what an already-unfolding event means, antecedent-focused, changing the feeling itself. Anchored by Gross (1998, cognitive change / reappraisal, generally the lower-cost strategy) and Gross (2015, its divergence from response-focused strategies).
- **[suppression](process_suppression.md)** (expression): Holding back the expression of a response already formed, response-focused, at a standing cost. Anchored by Gross (1998, response modulation / expressive suppression, its divergent -- costlier -- consequences for experience and physiology).
