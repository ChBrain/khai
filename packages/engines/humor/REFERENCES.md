# Humor: Reference

## Line of Work

Humor style as **position**: the default relational stance the persona takes toward comedy, who it is for, and at whose expense. The domain models the four humor styles along two axes (self-directed vs. other-directed; beneficial vs. detrimental) and what each style demands of the persona who holds it. It does not model the content of jokes, the comic craft, or the emotional experience of finding something funny.

The spine is Martin's Humor Styles Questionnaire (HSQ) framework: humor functions differently depending on whether it is directed at others or the self, and whether it serves the persona's wellbeing and relationships or undermines them. The four quadrants are empirically distinct and predict different outcomes for mental health, relationship quality, and social dominance.

## Origin

Humor psychology and the Humor Styles Questionnaire tradition.

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Comic craft**: The engine models the style's relational stance, not the persona's skill at constructing or delivering jokes. Comic timing, wit, and material belong to the Action and Projection in the persona's own files.
- **The emotional experience of amusement**: What it feels like to find something funny is delegated to the **emotion** engine; the humor engine models the stance, not the felt charge.
- **Cultural variation in what counts as funny**: The engine models the structural stance (who is the target; is the function beneficial or detrimental). What is considered humorous in a given culture or field belongs to the play's context and the persona's heritage.
- **Laughter as physiological response**: The bodily component of amusement is delegated to the **stress** and **emotion** engines; the humor engine models the relational position, not the nervous system.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[humor](position_humor.md)** (the anchor): Humor as position: the default stance toward comedy, its direction, and its social function. Anchored by Martin et al. (2003) -- the HSQ framework.
- **[affiliative](position_affiliative.md)** (expression): Humor directed at the room, benign and inclusive; used to bond and reduce tension. Anchored by Martin et al. (2003) -- high affiliative humor linked to wellbeing and relationship satisfaction.
- **[self-enhancing](position_self_enhancing.md)** (expression): Humor directed inward; a comic perspective on one's own situation maintained under stress. Anchored by Martin et al. (2003) -- self-enhancing humor linked to coping and emotional regulation.
- **[aggressive](position_aggressive.md)** (expression): Humor directed at others; teasing, ridicule, or put-down to establish or maintain position. Anchored by Martin et al. (2003) -- aggressive humor linked to dominance and lower agreeableness.
- **[self-defeating](position_self_defeating.md)** (expression): Humor that makes the self the target; self-deprecation to the point of ingratiation. Anchored by Martin et al. (2003) -- self-defeating humor linked to anxiety, loneliness, and reduced wellbeing.
