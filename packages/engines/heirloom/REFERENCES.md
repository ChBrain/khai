# Heirloom: Reference

## Line of Work

Heirloom as **piece**: an object deliberately withheld from exchange,
carrying a lineage's identity and authority forward across generations.
The domain does not model an ordinary valuable good, nor everyday gift
exchange; it models the possession set apart from both circuits -- the
thing a line keeps precisely so it can go on giving everything else away.

The spine is Weiner's "paradox of keeping-while-giving": a group secures
its rank and identity not only by what it circulates but by what it
refuses to circulate, and the possessions it keeps out of exchange are
what let its other gifts and exchanges mean anything at all. Mauss
supplies the deeper grammar of the gift -- the obligation to give,
receive, and reciprocate, and the recognition that some things remain
bound to their giver and are never fully alienated from them. Kopytoff
supplies the singularization: how a thing is pulled out of the sphere of
commodities and rendered, for as long as the line holds it, unsellable.

## Origin

The anthropology of exchange, applied directly as one piece and its
surrounding position and processes.

| Source             | Key Work                                                              | Scope                                                                                                                                                    |
| :----------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Annette Weiner** | _Inalienable Possessions: The Paradox of Keeping-While-Giving_ (1992) | Possessions deliberately kept out of exchange to reproduce a group's identity, rank, and cosmological authority over time.                               |
| **Marcel Mauss**   | _The Gift_ (1925)                                                     | The threefold obligation to give, receive, and reciprocate, and the recognition that certain gifts stay bound to their giver and resist full alienation. |
| **Igor Kopytoff**  | _"The Cultural Biography of Things"_ (1986)                           | Singularization: how an object is drawn out of the commodity sphere and rendered unexchangeable, and what re-commodifying it costs.                      |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Market value**: What a plain valuable good is worth to sell is the
  economic-capital or money engine's. Heirloom does not price the object;
  it models what happens once a thing is placed outside price.
- **Ordinary exchange**: The everyday circuit of gifts and reciprocity
  that Mauss also describes is not owned here. The engine carries only
  the possession set apart from that circuit -- the singular thing kept
  rather than moved.
- **The felt cost**: The grief of a loss, the shame of having sold what
  should have been kept -- those are the grief and shame engines'.
  Heirloom names the object and its custody; what holding or losing it
  feels like is delegated.
- **The lineage itself**: Which family or group holds the object, and
  the family cells that formed its members, is the heritage engine's (or
  the play's own content). Heirloom carries the kept thing and its
  custody, not the line that keeps it.

## Encoding

Source to constraint. A piece anchor (the kept object) with one position
(who holds it in trust) and two processes (its two fates: passed down,
or pulled out of the line).

- **[heirloom](piece_heirloom.md)** (the anchor): The object itself --
  load-bearing because it is precisely the thing the line has agreed not
  to exchange. Anchored by Weiner (inalienable possessions) and Mauss
  (the gift that stays bound to its giver).
- **[heir](position_heir.md)** (position): The stance of holding the
  object in trust rather than in ownership -- custody, and the standing
  it lends, for as long as it is kept. Anchored by Weiner (the keeper
  who reproduces identity through keeping) and Kopytoff (custodianship of
  the singularized thing).
- **[bequest](process_bequest.md)** (process): The object passing down
  the line, carried together with the account that makes it more than
  goods. Anchored by Mauss (the obligation that binds within a lineage)
  and Weiner (keeping-while-giving renewed each generation).
- **[alienation](process_alienation.md)** (process): The object crossing
  out of the line's custody -- sold, seized, or divested -- and what that
  crossing costs the line's identity. Anchored by Kopytoff
  (re-commodification, the singular thing returned to the market) and
  Weiner (the loss of authority when the kept possession is given up).
