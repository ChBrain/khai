# Piece: Heirloom

## Taxonomy

The paradox of keeping-while-giving / inalienable possessions (no khai file
of its own yet).

## Owner

- Project: khai
- Engine: heirloom

## Place

Not in the market -- set apart from everything else the line owns,
withheld from the circuit of gifts and sales that moves every other
possession along. It sits in the custody of one heir at a time, but
it belongs to the line as a whole: present at the ceremonies that
call for it, absent from the ledger that prices what may be sold.
Its place is defined by refusal. Nearly everything the line holds
may be exchanged; this may not, and the not is the point.

## Load Bearing

What it holds up is the line's claim to itself -- who they are, what
authority or rank the present generation carries, the descent that
makes that standing more than a story someone tells. Remove it and
the claim still exists in memory, but the thing that made it
visible and hard to dispute is gone. It legitimizes whoever holds it
-- a founding deed, an ancestral title, a name -- more than any
account recited without it ever could.

## Apparent

Rarely displayed at full value, and often plain enough that a
stranger passing it would not price it -- that is by design. Its
worth does not read off its market form; it reads off the occasion
it is brought out for and the account attached when it is. What
shows is custody and ceremony, not appraisal: who is trusted to hold
it, and the story recited each time it appears in the room.

## Yearbook

The arc runs generation to generation. Made or acquired at the
line's founding or at a defining moment, entrusted to a line of
heirs, brought out at thresholds -- successions, funerals, weddings
-- to renew the claim it carries. It peaks when the line is secure
and the current custody is uncontested. It fails when the object is
sold, lost, seized, or split among heirs who each want a piece of
it, and every one of those failures is a wound to the line's
identity, not merely a loss of property.
