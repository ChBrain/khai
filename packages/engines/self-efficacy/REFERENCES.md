# Self-Efficacy: Reference

## Line of Work

Self-efficacy as **position**: the persona's task-specific belief in their own
capability to organize and execute the actions a given attainment requires.
The domain does not model a global verdict on the persona's worth, nor the
force that actually mobilizes effort once the belief is set, nor the felt fear
that rides alongside a low read; it models the standing, revisable judgment of
"can I do this" carried into a specific task, and the source that judgment was
built from.

## Origin

Bandura's self-efficacy theory and its account of where the belief comes from.

| Source             | Key Work                                                                                      | Scope                                                                                                                                                          |
| ------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Albert Bandura** | _Self-Efficacy: The Exercise of Control_ (1997)                                               | The full theory: efficacy beliefs as task-specific, not global, and their four sources (mastery, vicarious, verbal persuasion, physiological/affective state). |
| **Albert Bandura** | _"Self-efficacy: Toward a unifying theory of behavioral change"_, Psychological Review (1977) | The founding formulation: efficacy expectations as distinct from outcome expectations, and mastery experience as the strongest source.                         |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **Whether the persona is worth anything** (global self-worth): Self-efficacy
  is a read on a specific task, never a verdict on the whole person. That
  broader, evaluative sense of worth **delegates to the self-esteem engine**.
  Self-efficacy answers "can I do this"; self-esteem answers "am I good."
- **What the persona is reaching for** (the target): Self-efficacy holds the
  belief that a goal is executable, not the goal itself. What the persona is
  oriented toward **delegates to the goal engine**.
- **What moves the persona to act** (the drive): Self-efficacy sets the
  ceiling on effort and persistence; the force that actually spends that
  ceiling toward a goal **delegates to the motivation engine**.
- **The felt fear of failure** (the affect): Self-efficacy is a cognitive
  appraisal of capability, not the anxious or confident feeling that
  accompanies it. The discrete, cause-bound feeling **delegates to the
  emotion engine**.

## Encoding

Source to constraint, per file.

- **[self-efficacy](position_self_efficacy.md)** (the anchor): a task-specific
  belief in one's own capability to execute, held and revised task by task
  rather than as one global measure. Anchored by Bandura (1997) on efficacy
  beliefs as task-specific, and Bandura (1977) on efficacy expectations as
  distinct from outcome expectations.
- **[mastery](position_mastery.md)** (expression): the belief built from the
  persona's own remembered successes -- the strongest and most durable
  source, resistant to an occasional setback. Anchored by Bandura (1997) on
  mastery experience as the most influential source of efficacy information.
- **[vicarious](position_vicarious.md)** (expression): the belief read off
  watching a similar other succeed at the same kind of task, borrowed rather
  than earned and weighted by how close the comparison holds. Anchored by
  Bandura (1997) on vicarious experience and modeling.
- **[persuasion](position_persuasion.md)** (expression): the belief lent by
  another's credible verbal encouragement, the most fragile of the three,
  propped on the speaker's credibility and apt to fold at the first real
  setback. Anchored by Bandura (1997) on verbal persuasion as a source weaker
  than mastery or vicarious experience.
