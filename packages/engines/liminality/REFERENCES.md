# Liminality: Reference

## Line of Work

Liminality as **place**: the individual threshold state between prior identity and what comes next. The domain models the liminal space as anchor (what it is to be between -- neither prior identity nor new identity; what the threshold shows, holds, offers, and withholds) and three expressions -- threshold (the crossing has just occurred; the prior status is freshly absent), suspension (held deep in the between; time passes differently; radical change is possible), emergence (the new identity is beginning to take shape at the edges) -- and what each state makes available or withholds.

The spine is the anthropology and sociology of liminality applied to individual experience: the between-state that van Gennep identified as the second phase of rites of passage is also a condition individuals inhabit outside formal ritual contexts -- in career transitions, identity reconstruction, bereavement, migration, illness, and any life passage where what was has ended before what will be has begun. Turner deepens the concept: liminality is productive precisely because the ordinary structures do not apply; the person who can tolerate the suspension is the one who can be genuinely transformed by it. Bridges supplies the application to individual life transitions: the liminal state (which he calls "the neutral zone") is not a gap to be crossed but a generative space to be inhabited.

## Origin

Anthropology of rites of passage, sociology of liminality, and the psychology of life transitions.

| Source                | Key Work                                                                                         | Scope                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| :-------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Arnold van Gennep** | _The Rites of Passage_ (1909; English trans. 1960)                                               | The tripartite structure of rites of passage: liminality as the threshold phase between separation (from prior status) and incorporation (into new status).                                                   |
| **Victor Turner**     | _The Ritual Process: Structure and Anti-Structure_ (1969)                                        | Liminality as productive suspension: the ordinary structures are dissolved; communitas and anti-structure make transformation possible. The liminal persona is betwixt and between.                           |
| **Victor Turner**     | "Betwixt and Between: The Liminal Period in Rites de Passage," in _The Forest of Symbols_ (1967) | Close analysis of what the liminal condition is: the stripped-down self; the absence of ordinary markers; what the threshold state shows and withholds.                                                       |
| **William Bridges**   | _Transitions: Making Sense of Life's Changes_ (1980)                                             | The psychological geography of individual life transitions: separation from the old; the neutral zone (suspension); the new beginning (emergence). The liminal space as generative, not merely uncomfortable. |

## Restrictions

What the engine refuses to model, and to whom it delegates.

- **The ritual that produced the liminality**: The collective rite of passage that structured the crossing belongs to the **ritual** engine. This engine models the individual's experience of being in-between, not the ceremony that put them there.
- **The collective experience of liminality**: When multiple people enter liminality together and form communitas, that collective dimension belongs to the **ritual** engine's liminality phase. This engine models individual threshold states.
- **Status before and after**: What the prior position was and what the new position will be belong to the **status**, **role**, and **position** engines respectively. This engine models only the between.
- **What the persona feels in the liminal state**: The disorientation, the exposure, the opening -- these belong to the **emotion** and **mood** engines. This engine models the space, not the felt charge.
