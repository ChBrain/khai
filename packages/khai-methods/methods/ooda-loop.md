A four-step decision cycle for acting inside a fast, contested situation: Observe the unfolding reality, Orient by making sense of it against experience and broken assumptions, Decide on a provisional course, and Act, then feed the result back into the next observation. The loop is continuous, not linear; its edge comes from cycling faster and orienting better than an opponent, so that by the time they react the situation has already moved on. Boyd held Orient to be the decisive step, the implicit reframing that shapes what everything else can see and do.

Developed by United States Air Force Colonel John Boyd from his study of air combat, and set out in his briefing _The Essence of Winning and Losing_ (first draft 1995, finalized 1996). Widely adopted beyond the military, in business, litigation, and cybersecurity, as a model for decision-making under competitive tempo.
