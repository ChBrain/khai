# Pitch: the key a run is played in

A **pitch** is the key you play a run in: the same fixed events, tuned to one
dominant tone. The play does not change (what happens is fixed); the pitch changes
how it is played and what the audience tastes. You **tune** a run to a pitch: pick
one dominant tenor to drive it. A run may shade into others, but the dominant
steers. A play bears some pitches better than others, so pick one its events can
carry. A different pitch is a different production of the same events, never a
rewrite. Note the pitch you tuned to when you capture.

A pitch has four facets (the khai pitch type, TO TUNE):

- **Tenor**: the dominant register the run is tuned to (the key its events are
  played in).
- **Undertow**: the counter-current beneath the tenor (the laugh inside the grief,
  the dread under the wonder) that keeps the tone from going flat.
- **Nerve**: the feeling the pitch is built to strike in the audience.
- **Echo**: the aftertaste it should leave once the run is over.

A starting palette of tenors (the dominant key; name your own as well):

- **grieving**: loss and compassion; the ache of what cannot be helped.
- **comic**: the ludicrous and the social; wit, deflation, the knot tied and untied.
- **tender**: longing and attraction; intimacy and the pull between figures.
- **furious**: heat and confrontation; the clash and the violence of will.
- **heroic**: courage and resolve; the cost met head on.
- **dread**: foreboding and the closing dark; the thing approaching.
- **abject**: the repellent and the unclean; recoil and what cannot be stomached.
- **wondrous**: awe and astonishment; the strange made present.
- **still**: quiet and release; acceptance, the calm after.

Picking a pitch is a directorial judgment, not a fact in the play; it is a
reading, never a change to what happens.
