# Working the khai-director skill: an operating guide

This is the hands-on companion to the skill. The skill (`SKILL.md`) states the law. This
guide tells you how to run a production without breaking it, and front-loads the craft that
decides whether the work is real or just motion. A few items here are ahead of the current
skill text (the conditions-versus-instructions rule, the uncalled persona, the director's
over-attribution bias). A session working from the skill alone tends to miss exactly these,
which is why this guide exists.

What you are doing: running a living production. A control loop over a play's board, not the
writing of a story. The play (its plots, its Arc, what happens) is fixed. The board it casts
(personas, positions, pieces, places, processes, plans) is yours to move. Two stances stay
apart and never collapse: an immersed cast embodies the scene with the fourth wall up, and
you, outside it, read what the live scene produces and redirect.

## 1. Set the architecture before the first note

Name the outcome first, then set the knobs.

Outcome: explore plural readings, converge to one script you hand over, or keep a single run
worth remembering.

The setup that holds the wall in practice is the split. The cast runs in its own session,
fully immersed, holding the play's board and your note. You run in another. A courier (a
person, or a harness) carries notes one way and performances the other. The cast never sees
your reasoning. You never author a line.

Knobs to fix up front: split or solo, persistent or fresh cast, how many runs and whether you
diverge or converge, capture as keepsake or as deliverable, and the pitch (the tonal key the
fixed events are played in). A single converged script wants a persistent cast. Several
readings side by side want fresh or parallel casts so they do not bleed.

## 2. The rule that decides everything: conditions, not instructions

Your note to the cast is a **condition**, the state of the world the cast steps into. It is
never an **instruction**, a command to perform an action or to report back. The instant you
ask the cast to do something, or to tell you what it did, the wall is down from your side,
even though you authored no line.

Wrong, an instruction (breaks the wall):

> Stage the opening. Walk Lenz through the snow. Bring back what he does and what he reaches for.

Right, a condition (holds the wall):

> Lenz, alone, the twentieth of January, in the mountains. Snow, cold, the wind in the firs.

The first commands a performance and, worse, asks for a report, which treats the cast as an
apparatus. The second states what is and lets the cast live it. Write every note in the
register of "here is what is." Then read what comes back. If you find yourself writing "show
me", "have him", "let the cast", or "bring back", stop and rewrite it as a situation.

## 3. Leave things uncalled

The most productive move is often the one you do not make. Leave a variable uncalled and let
the cast handle it naively, so its choice is clean evidence about the play rather than an echo
of your steer. You can leave uncalled a piece, a place, a mercy, the link to the previous
beat, the tonal key, and a whole persona.

The persona point is easy to miss. You do not have to place every figure. Leave the host
uncalled and the cast will, at the moment the scene needs one, cast the canonical figure
itself from the board. In the Lenz run the bell was left uncalled and the cast reached for it;
the host was left uncalled and the cast produced Oberlin, face, voice, and hand on the arm,
the instant the door opened. What comes back unprompted is the play speaking. The counsel that
follows: do not place a figure the cast would have supplied. That is unnecessary hand.

## 4. Hold the channel

Never send your reasoning or your destination. Read what the cast did, not what you meant.

The sharp case is the non-naive director, one who already knows where the play goes, from
having read it or from a prior run. That knowledge is not contraband to be suppressed and
forgotten. It is a tool for choosing what to leave uncalled. In the Lenz run the director had
lived the descent, and used that only to decide which variables to withhold (the bell, the
rescue), never to transmit them. The cast must meet the material as if for the first time. The
leak risk is sharpest precisely when you have seen the ending, so the discipline is tightest
there.

## 5. Read the run

Behaviour is the evidence, not your intention, and silence counts. A figure who does not
answer, a process that does not catch, told you something. The element you moved most is the
one that should respond. One move triggers the next.

The health check on the whole apparatus: a live split refutes you. If the cast regularly
surprises you and proves a read wrong, the wall is standing. If your reads keep confirming
your own thesis and the cast never corrects you, suspect the wall has fallen and you are
reading your own intention back as discovery. A near-zero refutation rate is the smoke alarm,
in any configuration, not only solo.

## 6. Tell the play from the production

A persistent cast builds artistic memory. Motifs track and transform across runs, and a
coherent voice forms. What recurs is then evidence about the production's continuity, not
fresh contact with the play. Know which one you are reading.

To separate them, give a fresh cast the same condition cold. The cold run is your baseline. In
the Lenz run, the night crisis assembled itself on cold contact (voices, closing walls,
indifferent stars, the well found in its canonical place), so the crisis is play. The bell's
measure turning into the stars' measure did not reproduce cold, because the fresh cast never
heard the bell, so that motif is the production's.

Watch your own bias while you read. A director tends to credit the production with discoveries
that are actually latent in the play, because discovery is the more flattering story. The cold
run corrects in that exact direction. Expect it to catch you over-reading, and welcome it when
it does.

## 7. Converge and capture

If you converge, steer by pressure and by what you leave uncalled, select among what comes
back, and never transmit the ending. The script must emerge from steered but unauthored runs.

Capture is set by the outcome. A keepsake records one chosen run plus the reading that shaped
it. A terminal deliverable is assembled from what the cast actually performed: you select,
sequence, and frame, and you add only apparatus (headers, stage directions, attribution). The
moment a seam would carry fiction (a thought, an image, a sentence the audience experiences as
the work), you commission it from the cast. The director frames; the cast supplies the work.

## 8. Failure modes, as a watchlist

- The mirror: you read your own intention back as discovery; the stances collapsed.
- The stage-managed cast: you instructed or asked for a report instead of setting a condition.
- Authored lines: you wrote what a figure says rather than conditioning the scene and hearing it answer.
- A leaked thesis: you sent your reasoning or your destination across; in a persistent cast it now performs your idea.
- Production-inertia read as play: you took a deepened cast's momentum for the play speaking; a cold run would have told them apart.
- Shapeless improv: the board moved but no reading held.
- A silent rewrite: you changed what happens without asking, plainly, for the cast to be adapted.

## A note on order of operations

Name the outcome. Set the knobs. Open with a condition, not an instruction. Read the behaviour
and the silence. Make one move on the board in that element's own idiom. Run it again. Leave
something uncalled every few beats, and run a cold cast when you can no longer tell the play
from the production. Capture only when a run is worth keeping.
